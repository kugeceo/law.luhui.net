CASE 10

CLAIM UNDER WARRANTY AFTER EXPIRY OF PERIOD OF INSPECTION
 　　How should the quality standards for goods under contract be determined when the buyer and the seller disagree in their understanding of the specifications of the goods?  Is the seller still liable to pay damages when the goods are confirmed upon inspection to be faulty even though the contractual inspection period has lapsed (but the goods are still under warranty)?
 
THE ARBITRATION DECISION
 
I.                  Facts of the Case
 
　　On 14 February 1989, the claimant (the buyer) and the respondent (the seller) signed a purchase contract No. (89) All-001 in Shenzhen for the supply of 10,000 glass panes for use in induction cookers. The measurements of the panes were to be 272mm x 272mm x 3.8mm. The specifications were to be as detailed in the annex to the contract. The unit price for the panes was US$4.85, CIF Shenzhen. The total cost of the goods under the contract was US$48,500. Of the goods to be supplied, 4,000 pieces were to be delivered at the end of March 1989, and 6,000 pieces were to be delivered in June 1989. The buyer would pay for the goods in two installments by irrevocable L/Cs payable on sight. An L/C with the seller as beneficiary was to be issued through the Shenzhen Branch of the Bank of China one month before delivery.
　Clause 14 of the contract stipulated that upon arrival of the goods at the port of destination, the buyer could appoint the China Commodities Inspection Bureau to reinspect the goods. If any damage to, fault or non-conformance with the specifications and quantity of the goods under the contract was found, the buyer could claim damages from the seller within 90 days of the goods arriving at the port of destination on the strength of the certificate of inspection issued by the China Commodities Inspection Bureau.
　Clause 13 of the contract provided that the warranty period for the quality of the goods extended to 12 months after the goods arrived at the port of destination. During the warranty period, the seller would be liable for any defects in the goods which resulted from faults in design and manufacture.
　After the contract was signed, the respondent delivered to the claimant the first batch of 4,000 glass panes for use in induction cookers on 2 May 1989. On 12 May 1989, the claimant refused to pay for the goods on the grounds that the certificate of quality did not fulfill the requirements and requested that the respondent issue another certificate of quality. On 16 May 1989, the respondent provided a certificate of quality issued through the bank. The claimant then made the payment of US$19,400 for the goods on 10 June 1989.
　After accepting delivery, the claimant immediately sent the goods to its client without carrying out a commodities inspection on the goods. Upon testing, the client found that the material used in the batch of glass panes for the ‘Xin Le’ brand of induction cookers was inconsistent with the requirements for these panes under the contract and asked the claimant to withdraw the goods and pay damages. On　8 December 1989, the claimant sent a fax to the respondent requesting negotiations on the quality problem of the glass panes pursuant to the warranty clause (clause 13) of the contract. When negotiations failed to resolve the dispute, on 30 May 1990 the claimant applied to the Commission for arbitration.
 　In its application for arbitration, the claimant requested that the respondent compensate the claimant for economic losses suffered by reason of the respondent's breach of the purchase contract totalling US$19,400 (Rmb170, 417.90).
　
The major points of dispute and the arguments of the parties were as follows:
 
　1. The claimant submitted that it was specified in the contract that, ‘the specifications for the goods are to be as detailed in the annex to the contract’. The annex to the contract was the annex produced by the claimant as evidence. The annex contained the requirements as to the material, measurements and technical specifications of the goods.
The respondent held that the `annex to the contract' produced by the claimant could not be considered the annex to the contract because the claimant and the respondent, parties to the contract, had not signed the document to certify that it was an integral part of the contract.
The respondent submitted that on the day of conclusion of the contract, there was no other document for the respondent to take back to Hong Kong. After about 15 days, the claimant requested that the respondent provide the name and technical data of glass panes for induction cookers. The respondent informed the claimant of the measurements and other specifications of these glass panes. It was only after sending this information that the respondent received the formal contract from the claimant.
The respondent also submitted that it was only after it provided the sample product to the claimant for confirmation and acceptance that it ordered the goods from Taiwan. The goods it delivered conformed exactly to the contracted requirements for the goods and to the accepted sample and did not differ from the standards for the panes agreed to by the parties to the contract.
2 Should the ‘glass panes for use in induction cookers’ under the contract be micro- crystal glass or float steel glass? The claimant submitted that it (and its client) needed micro-crystal glass and not float steel glass panes. The respondent was well aware of this. Until the arbitration hearing, the respondent considered that the ‘glass panes for use in induction cookers’ it provided pursuant to the contract were micro-crystal glass. This was shown by the specification of this in the carrier's receipt of the goods. However, the goods it actually delivered were of steel glass. One of the most convincing grounds for the claimant's claim on 8 December 1989 for damages from the respondent was that the goods were not in crystal form and were not micro-crystal glass. The respondent never disputed this point.

The respondent submitted that evidence showed that the goods required by the claimant were not micro-crystal glass but float steel glass, based on the following:
　　1. The description of the goods in the contract said ‘glass panes for use in induction cookers’, micro-crystal glass was not specified.
　　2. Several days after the conclusion of the contract, the respondent sent to the claimant by fax the specifications and technical data of the glass panes in English provided by the manufacturer. The description of the product was float steel glass and the claimant raised no objection to this after it received the specifications:
　　3. After receiving the English specifications, the claimant issued an L/C which also specified that it was purchasing 4,000 pieces of float steel glass.
　　4. The ultimate certificate of quality provided by the manufacturer of the respondent also specified that the goods were float steel glass. This certificate was accepted by the claimant, who then paid for the goods. The actions of the claimant showed it was aware that it was purchasing float steel glass. It was impossible for it to now claim compensation on the grounds that the goods supplied were not micro- crystal glass.
　　5.     There were no grounds for the claimant's claim that the Chinese wording ‘micro- crystal glass’ was written by the respondent on the carrier's receipt. The goods were transported from Taiwan via Hong Kong to Shenzhen. All transportation and re-export formalities were completed by the freight company engaged by the Taiwanese manufacturer; the respondent did not handle the transportation of the goods directly. The ‘carrier's receipt’ was completed on 5 May 1989, whereas the goods had already arrived at Shenzhen on 2 May 1989 (the carrier's receipt also specified that the date of departure of the truck was 2 May 1989). It was obvious that the carrier's receipt was completed after the goods had arrived at Shenzhen and was not completed by the respondent in Hong Kong. From the remark ‘Incomplete documents. Temporary storage at the Customs Office’ at the end of the carrier's receipt, it could be deduced that after the goods arrived at Shenzhen, some officers in China filled in the carrier's receipt. It should be noted that during the respondent's performance of the contract, apart from the English name of the goods, only the wording ‘glass panes for use in induction cookers’ was used in all fax messages or letters as the Chinese description of the goods. This description was the same as that used in the contract. The Chinese word ‘micro- crystal glass’ had never been used.
　　The claimant submitted that the subject matter of the contract, ‘glass panes for use in induction cookers’, was custom-made for the user. The respondent was well aware that the goods it provided had not only to fulfill basic requirements of the goods for induction cookers but also serve the specific purpose of being used only in induction cookers produced by the client. However, the goods supplied by the respondent did not meet the basic requirements nor the specific purpose of the goods. The goods did not even satisfy the specifications for the goods detailed on the certificate of quality provided by the respondent (upon testing after assembly, the glass panes shattered at 2000C to 3000C). The goods could not, therefore, be used in production and the client was forced to stop production, resulting in great losses on the part of various Chinese parties involved in the import of this product.
　　The respondent submitted that the claimant had accepted the certificate of quality provided by the manufacturer and had made payment. If the claimant considered that the goods and the certificate of quality provided by the respondent did not conform to the requirements for the goods under the contract, it should have immediately arranged for reinspection of the goods. As the claimant did not send the goods to the commodities inspection authority for inspection within the 90-day period stipulated in the contract, it should be deemed to have relinquished its right to inspect the goods and to have accepted the goods. It had also lost its right to reject the goods and its right to make a claim.
　　The respondent also considered that the claimant could not resort to clause 13 of the contract regarding the warranty period of 12 months after arrival of the goods at the port of destination. The claimant's claim was that the material used in the batch of glass panes for induction cookers was not of the micro-crystal glass that it required. Whether the glass panes were micro-crystal glass was a question of material used and not related to faulty design or manufacture. Evidence in China and from overseas showed that the material for glass panes for use in induction cookers was not restricted to micro-crystal glass. The contract in this case related to ‘glass panes for use in induction cookers’ and did not specify the material to be used. Therefore, it was unreasonable for the claimant to present the material used as a technical issue on which damages could be claimed.

II. Views of the Arbitration Tribunal
 　　Based on the above facts, arguments of the parties and the report by the inspection authority, the arbitration tribunal made the following analysis and judgment:
　　1. According to the purchase contract and the evidence submitted by the parties, the respondent should supply to the claimant glass panes for use in induction cookers of merchantable quality. The parties differed in their understanding of whether the material for the glass panes should be micro-crystal glass or float steel glass. However, neither party produced sufficient evidence to convince the arbitration tribunal of its claim.
　　2. Regarding performance of the contract, the respondent delivered the first batch of 4,000 pieces of glass panes for induction cookers to the Customs Office on 2 May 1989. As the documents were incomplete, the goods were temporarily stored at the Customs Office. On 12 May 1989, the claimant refused to pay for the goods because the certificate of quality did not satisfy requirements. On 16 May 1989, the respondent resubmitted a certificate of quality through the bank. Thereafter, the claimant accepted the goods and made payment. The new certificate of quality was accepted by both parties. The arbitration tribunal held that the goods supplied by the respondent should conform to the requirements of the certificate of quality.
　　According to the inspection report issued by the glass laboratory of the China Commodities Inspection Bureau, the goods supplied by the respondent did not fulfill the requirements for the good specified in the certificate of quality it issued. Of the eight requirements specified in the certificate of quality, it was clearly stated in the inspection report that the goods did not fulfill the requirements of items 4 and 6. Item 4 of the certificate of quality required that the bending strength should be 1,500kg / lcm2 whereas the results in the inspection report were 1,022.34, 1,048.52 and 1,324.90kg / cm2. The glass panes did not meet the bending strength requirement. Item 6 of the certificate of quality required that the impact resistance should be 2/12 (ie, only two pieces out of 12 should break) upon impact of a 227g steel ball falling from a height of 1.5m. The inspection report showed that the glass panes were all broken after impact and therefore did not meet the requirements.
　　3. The respondent claimed that the goods it supplied agreed with the sample under the contract. However, the respondent could not provide a sample sealed and confirmed by both parties. Therefore, the arbitration tribunal considered that the claim of the respondent that the goods supplied under the contract complied with the sample could not be upheld. Only the technical parameters specified in the certificate of quality confirmed by both parties could be used as the basis for judging whether the goods conformed to the requirements for the goods under the contract.
　　4. According to clause 13 of the contract the warranty period for the goods was 12 months after the goods arrived at the port of destination. After it received the goods in May 1989, the claimant claimed compensation from the respondent based on the quality of the goods in December 1989. The arbitration tribunal held that the claimant's claim was raised within the warranty period provided for in the contract.
　　The respondent submitted that the claimant's failure to send the goods to the commodities inspection bureau for inspection within the 90-day period of claim should be deemed as relinquishment of its right to inspect the goods. As a result, the claimant also lost its right to reject the goods and its right to make a claim. The arbitration tribunal considered that the 90-day inspection period after the arrival of the goods at the port of destination stipulated in clause 14 of the contract applied to the measurements and quantity of the goods only. Clause 13 on the 12-month warranty period, on the other hand, applied to defects related to the properties of the goods. Therefore, the failure on the part of the claimant to send the goods for inspection pursuant to clause 14 did not relieve the respondent of its obligation to warrant the quality of the goods pursuant to clause 13.
　　Based on the above facts and analysis, the arbitration tribunal considered that the dispute in this case arose because the goods supplied by the respondent did not conform to the quality standard for the goods agreed to by the parties to the contract. The respondent should be liable for this and compensate the claimant accordingly. The claimant's claim for the payment plus interest was reasonable and should be awarded. The other claims submitted by the claimant, however, had no support and would not be granted.

II.               The Decision
　　Based on the above facts, analysis and judgment, the arbitration tribunal decided as follows:
　　1. The respondent shall, within 40 days of the date of this award, return to the claimant the US$19,400 paid for the goods, plus interest of US$4,737.90 (calculated at the rate of eight per cent per annum from 10 June 1989 to 29 June 1992). Overdue interest of ten per cent per annum will be imposed in case of late payment.
　　2. Within 40 days of the date of this award, the claimant and the respondent shall agree on a time for the return of the goods. The claimant shall complete the customs declaration formalities and send the goods supplied by the respondent to the respondent at the Customs Office where the respondent will takeaway the goods. If the respondent does not take the goods at the time agreed upon by the parties, the goods will be disposed of by the claimant.
　3. The other claims of the claimant shall be dismissed.
　4. The arbitration fees of US$800 and Rmb6, 000 and the handling fee's of Rmb4, 000 shall be borne as follows: the respondent shall bear the arbitration fees of US$800 and the handling fees of Rmb4,000 and the claimant shall bear the arbitration fees of Rmb6,000.
 
DISCUSSION
There are three main areas of dispute in this case:
　　1. What goods were required by the claimant (the buyer)? What goods should the respondent (the seller) deliver? Should the ‘glass panes for use in induction cookers’ be ‘micro-crystal glass’ or ‘float steel glass’?
　　2. Should the quality of the goods be judged against the sample or against the certificate of quality?
　　3． Must the claim for compensation on the quality of the goods be submitted within 90 days of the arrival of the goods as provided for in the contract or within the 12-month warranty period, also provided for in the contract?
　　In international trade, the parties may agree to supply goods according to descriptions of goods or according to a sample or other standards. If there is an agreement between the parties, the quality and specifications of the goods delivered by the seller must conform to the standards agreed between the parties. If there is no agreement, the goods supplied by the seller should be fit for the purpose of the goods which is known or should be known by the parties at the time of the conclusion of the contract. If the goods are general goods, they should be fit for their usual purpose.
　 In their contract, the parties did not specify whether the goods to be supplied were to be micro-crystal glass or float steel glass. It was specified, however, that the goods to be delivered by the seller should be ‘glass panes for use in induction cookers’. This shows that the parties knew at the time of concluding the contract that the goods under the contract had a specific purpose, ie, the glass panes were for use in the manufacture of induction cookers. Regarding the dispute of the parties on the description and quality. of the goods delivered, the arbitration tribunal determined, according to the contract and the evidence submitted by the parties, that the respondent (the seller) should supply to the claimant (the buyer) ‘glass panes for use in induction cookers’ of merchantable quality.
　　During arbitration, the respondent submitted that the sample of the goods had been confirmed and accepted by the claimant before the contact was concluded, and the goods supplied by the respondent conformed to the sample. However, the respondent did not produce the sample sealed and confirmed by the parties to support its claim. Under such circumstances, it is natural for the arbitration tribunal to reject the respondent's claim. The claimant, on the other hand, submitted to the arbitration tribunal an annex to the contract and a certificate of quality issued by the respondent and requested that these be used as the bases for assessment of the quality of the goods. According to the contract, the measurements of the goods were to be 272mmx 272mm x 3.Smm; the specifications of the goods were to be as detailed in the annex to the contract. The contract should therefore have an annex on the basis of which the quality of the goods delivered could be assessed. The parties did not sign or seal the document, however, and the respondent denied the authenticity and validity of such annex. The arbitration tribunal was not, therefore, able to recognise the value of the annex as evidence to the case. Fortunately, the claimant presented to the arbitration tribunal a certificate of quality issued by the respondent which listed certain quality standards over which the parties had no dispute. Therefore, the arbitration tribunal determined that the goods supplied by the respondent should meet the quality standards specified in this certificate of quality. As confirmed by the inspection of the state commodities inspection authority, the quality of the goods supplied by the respondent did not conform to the standards specified in the certificate of quality that the respondent issued. This served as a reliable basis on which the arbitration tribunal could judge the liability of the respondent for breach of contract.
　　Regarding the relationship between the 90-day period of claim and the 12-month warranty period, the respondent argued that the claimant must send the goods for inspection and raise any claims for compensation on the quality of the goods within 90 days as agreed in the contract. If this was to hold, the parties need not have agreed to the 12-month warranty period for the quality of the goods. The agreement on the 12-month warranty period shows that the goods supplied by the respondent had a special purpose. In fact, the 90-day period for inspection and claim agreed to in clause 14 of the contract applied to the measurements, quantity and condition of the goods, whereas the 12-month warranty period provided for in clause 13 of the contract applied to the intrinsic quality of the goods. The failure on the part of the claimant to inspect and claim compensation within the time period specified in clause 14 did not relieve the respondent of its obligation, to uphold the warranty on the properties of the goods pursuant to clause 13 of the contract.
　　When drafting a contract, the buyer and the seller should specify the required description, specifications and quality of the goods in the contract to avoid disputes similar to the dispute in this case. If the parties agree to supply the goods against sample, a sample must be sealed and kept by either or both parties to be used as the basis for examination of the goods supplied. If there is an annex to the contract, the parties crystal glass. The respondent never disputed this point. The respondent submitted that evidence showed that the goods required by the ciure or seal. If the goods to be supplied are for a special purpose (as in this case), different periods of claim for the quality and properties of the goods should be specified.
