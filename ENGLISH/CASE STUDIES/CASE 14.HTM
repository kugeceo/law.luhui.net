CASE 14

ESTABLISHED PRACTICE BETWEEN PARTIES BINDING
 　　The sale and purchase contract between the parties in this case does not make specific provision for the quality standards for the goods. The parties carry out the transaction following trading practices established between them. To what extent and how can it be determined whether established practice followed in the performance of a contract are binding on the parties who adopt these practices? Can a representative of the buyer bind the company by his actions?
 
 
THE ARBITRATION DECISION
 
I. Facts of the Case
　　On 26 September 1989, the claimant and the respondent signed contract No. QFD890011, which provided that the claimant (the seller) was to produce for the respondent (the buyer) 11,000 jackets, Nos. 2811 and 2812, as described in a production list. The total price of the goods was US$91,300, FOB Quanzhou. The goods were to be delivered on 25 October 1989. The seller warranted the conformance of the quality, specifications and technical details of the goods to the requirements of the contract. Any consequences arising from non-conformance of the goods would be the seller's responsibility. The buyer was to issue an irrevocable L/C payable on sight to the seller before 30 September 1989. Accessories under the contract would be provided by the buyer. If these accessories did not reach Quanzhou before 30 September 1989, delivery could be postponed accordingly.
　　In its application for arbitration, the claimant claimed that on 4 October 1989, it sent the fabrics to be used in production of the jackets to Mr. Wu; manager of the garment department of the respondent, for confirmation. The claimant then immediately arranged for production of the goods. Following production, Mr. Yang, director of the respondent, made a phone call to the claimant refusing to perform the contract on grounds of unacceptable fabrics. Despite the claimant's repeated requests, the respondent insisted that it would neither inspect nor accept the goods. As a result, the 11,000 jackets worth a total of Rmb505, 560 were put in stock and the claimant, due to the non-performance of the contract by the respondent, suffered heavy economic losses. In its application to the arbitration tribunal, the claimant requested that the respondent immediately compensate it Rmb505, 560 being losses it suffered as a result of the respondent's non-performance of the contract, plus interest and storage expenses incurred by the claimant as a result.
　　In its defence, the respondent submitted that the contract was a processing contract negotiated by the claimant and NH Company of the US. The respondent was the middleman. Both the claimant and the respondent were aware that the goods would finally be delivered to NH Company. Therefore, the style, price, quantity and quality of the goods had to be confirmed by NH Company. After entering into the contract, the respondent provided the sample fabrics, but the claimant sent unsuitable fabrics to the factory for processing. On 10 October 1989, the respondent noted that the color of the fabrics used by the claimant did not match the sample and immediately informed the claimant that the goods could not be accepted. However, having cut the fabrics and sent them to the factory, the claimant continued to produce the goods. On 16 October 1989, the claimant sent sample fabrics to the respondent. The respondent replied by fax that the sample fabrics had been received and sent on to NH Company. NH Company confirmed that these samples did not conform to the sample of the first batch of goods and that these fabrics could not be used. The respondent asked the claimant to find a solution to this problem or NH Company would not accept the goods. The claimant made no move other than to continue production. The jackets produced included some of fabrics that were striped and some of different colors. The workmanship was poor and the respondent could not accept them.
　　The respondent also claimed that the `confirmation produced by Mr. Wu submitted by the claimant to the arbitration tribunal was just a piece of paper because Mr. Wu was an ordinary officer and not the manager of the garment department of the respondent. The respondent never authorised Mr. Wu to sign any `confirmation card'.Further; the confirmation card submitted by the claimant was not sealed, did not bear the signatures of the parties and was therefore invalid.
　　The respondent also made a counterclaim for the return by the claimant of accessories worth HK$545,833.43. However, because the respondent had not completed the counterclaim procedures pursuant to the Arbitration Rules of CIETAC, the Shenzhen Branch of the Commission did not accept its counterclaim.

II. Views of the Arbitration Tribunal
　　The arbitration tribunal considered the liabilities of the parties based on its investigation.
　　1. The dispute between the parties centred on the quality of the goods. Clause 6 of the contract on ‘Quality’ stated, ‘The seller (the claimant) warrants the conformance of the quality, specifications and technical details of the goods with the requirements for these under the contract (or with the sample provided by the seller). In the event of non-conformance of the goods, the seller shall be responsible for all consequences of this non-conformance.’ However, the column, ‘description and specifications’ in the contract stated only, `refer to the production list'. According to the materials presented to the tribunal by the claimant, the ‘production list’ was the confirmation of purchase signed by the respondent and a company in Quanzhou (the processing factory). The column, ‘description and specifications of goods’ stated only `jackets, N2811, N2812'. No detailed provisions for the quality and specifications of the goods were included. The L/C issued by the respondent also made no mention of the sample fabrics, now in dispute, in the description of the goods.
　　During performance of the contract, the parties sought confirmation of the sample fabrics after signing the contract. According to the information provided by the claimant, the claimant sent the sample fabrics for the jackets to Mr. Wu of the respondent for confirmation. On 4 October 1989, Mr. Wu signed a confirmation letter using the letterhead of ‘Fujian Province Foreign Trade Corporation -Quanzhou Branch’, to which the sample fabrics were attached. The claimant then sent the cut fabrics to the factory for production. In its defence, the respondent denied that Mr. Wu was the manager of its garment department and refused to recognise the sample fabrics confirmed by him: However; the name card handed to the claimant by Mr. Wu showed the title, Garment Department Assistant Manager of the respondent. Moreover; Mr. Wu was stationed in Quanzhou and was responsible for supervising garment production. The claimant therefore had reason to believe that Mr. Wu had confirmed the sample fabrics in the capacity of a representative of the respondent. The respondent claimed that it had supplied its sample fabrics to the claimant after signing the contract. The respondent actually submitted the sample fabrics only after the arbitration hearing and the sample submitted to the arbitration tribunal bore no signature to confirm acceptance of the fabrics. Therefore, the arbitration tribunal held that the sample fabrics signed by Mr. Wu should be the fabrics that it would use as the basis for its judgment.
　　After the claimant cut the fabrics (which conformed to the sample fabrics) and started production, the respondent telephoned the claimant on 10 October 1989 informing it that the blue sample could not be accepted (no objection was raised to the other fabrics). On 16 October 1989, it sent a further fax message informing the claimant that it would not accept any of the samples because the respondent's buyer refused to accept the goods. It was stipulated in the contract that the delivery date for the goods was 25 October 1989. The respondent's refusal to accept the goods only eight days before the date of delivery, despite its previous confirmation of the samples, was a breach of the agreement between the claimant and the respondent:
　　2. The trading terms agreed in the contract were FOB Quanzhou. Under these terms, the principal obligations of the buyer were: to hire a vessel or to book the necessary shipping space, to inform the seller of the name of the vessel and the shipping date, and to bear all expenses and risks related to the goods after they were loaded. In an FOB contract, therefore, it is a precondition of the seller's shipping of the goods that the buyer hire the vessel or book the shipping space. In this case, by refusing to hire a ship or to book necessary shipping space to take delivery of the goods, the respondent, as the buyer, did not fulfill its obligationsunder the contract and should be liable for the breach of contract.
　　3. After examining the evidence submitted by the claimant, the arbitration tribunal was of the view that the claimant had grounds for claiming damages for the losses it suffered due to its enforced storage of the finished goods. As the jackets were specially produced to cater to the sales needs of the respondent, it was difficult for the claimant to reduce its losses by selling the finished goods. Therefore it was held that the amount of damages claimed by the claimant and the interest claimed thereon were reasonable. The respondent should, within a reasonable period of time after settling the damages, take delivery of the goods pursuant to the contract.
　　The arbitration tribunal would not consider the claimant's claim for the storage costs because the claimant provided insufficient evidence to support it.


III. The Decision
Based on the above analysis, the arbitration tribunal decided as follows:
　　1. The respondent shall, within 30 days of the date of this award, pay to the claimant Rmb505,560 plus interest on this amount of Rmb138,000, accruing from 28 October 1989 to the date of the award. Further interest shall be imposed on any amount that is not paid by this date.
　　2. Following settlement in full by the respondent of the amount in the above item, the claimant shall assist the respondent in taking the goods.
　　3. The claimant's claim for storage costs is dismissed.
　　4. The arbitration and handling fees for the case shall be borne by the respondent.
This decision shall be final.

DISCUSSION
　　The major issue in this case is whether established practice followed by parties is binding on them when the contract does not otherwise make specific provisions for the quality of the goods.
　　Under a sale of goods contract, the goods delivered by the seller must conform to the quality agreed to between the parties under the contract. This is a principle recognised by the laws of all countries. Consequently, the contract must be specific, detailed and clear in its description of the quality of the goods. If the contract does not specify the quality of the goods or does not make clear specifications for the quality of the goods, usual industry practice or the usual practice in past transactions between the buyer and the seller should be followed.
　　The analysis and judgment of the arbitration tribunal showed that the terms of the contract were extremely simple. It only stated the number of the goods as N2811 and N2812. The terms of the contract were left simple because the parties had earlier signed a principal contract for the sale and purchase of 64,000 waterproof jackets, of which the majority had been delivered. The goods N2811 and N2812 were part of this principal contract, but the parties were to confirm the price for these two batches of goods later. Therefore, they signed the contract now in dispute, however, the terms of the principal contract for the sale and purchase of 64,000 jackets were also very simple. There was no description of the specifications and materials for the goods. The L/C issued by the respondent carried a more detailed description:
　　Goods: Pure cotton jackets for men and children, made of dyed water-rinsed cloth. Material: Pure cotton canvas on the outer surface, 72 x 40,21f 10, dyed; lining for the body: pure cotton flannel imprinted with a map, 42 x 44, 24/13; lining for the sleeves: 100% nylon taffeta, 190 tonnes, matching colors; stuffing: 8 ounces of soft polythene for the body, 6 ounces of soft polythene for the sleeves. The model number for N2812 is 8633. 5,000  pieces for children.
　　A dispute arose between the parties over the quality of the outer material of the jackets. There was only a general description of the materials to be used and general specifications of the materials in the L/C. It was difficult, on the basis of this meager description, to determine whether the goods delivered by the seller conformed to the requirements of the L/C. The reason the respondent did not accept the goods was the non-conformance of the materials with the materials that had been agreed upon according to a sample, ie, the color was different from the color code and there were stripes on the fabrics. The dispute then centred on the sample fabrics. Under the contract and in the L/C, however, there was no requirement for delivery against sample.
　　After the parties entered into the contract, the claimant sent a sample of the fabrics for the jackets to Mr. Wu of the respondent for confirmation of the quality. The claimant attached three small pieces of these fabrics to a piece of paper bearing the letterhead, ‘Fujian Province Foreign Trade Corporation - Quanzhou Branch’, writing next to them the words ‘dark grey, blue and light olive’. Another line read ‘brown: as provided before’. Below the first line of the letter, were the specifications ‘21 S/2 x 10S/ 1, 5140 canvas’. Mr. Wu signed this letter and wrote the date, which was 4 October 1989, below his signature.
　　In its defence, the respondent objected to the confirmation card signed by Mr. Wu because Mr. Wu was only an ordinary officer and not the manager of the garment department. The respondent had never authorised Mr. Wu to sign the confirmation and he did not, therefore, have the power to confirm the sample on behalf of the respondent. As the confirmation card did not bear the signatures of the two parties and had not been immediately sealed, it was just apiece of paper and had no official value. Here the representative capacity of Mr. Wu came into question. Did he have the authority to confirm the sample? It was true that he did not have a letter of authorization signed by the respondent. However, judging from the circumstances of the entire transaction, the contracting parties had, before this transaction, already completed several other transactions that also involved jackets in performance of the principal contract for 64,000 jackets. From the outset, the respondent had appointed representatives to be stationed at the factories to supervise the production of the garments. Mr. Wu was one of these, representatives. The parties' usual practice had been for the representative of the respondent stationed at the processing factory to confirm the sample fabric before the materials were used for production. Upon completion of each batch of garments, the products could only be delivered after being inspected and passed by these representatives. Several transactions involving jackets between the parties were conducted in this manner, and the respondent had never disputed the arrangement.
　　Legally speaking, the action of the respondent comprised ‘agency by estoppel’. In other words, when A shows by its actions that B has the capacity as its agent and C believes on this basis that B has the capacity as the agent' of A and enters into an agreement with B, A cannot deny the agreement. Moreover; the name card given by Mr. Wu to the claimant actually showed that Mr. Wu was the assistant manager of the garment department of the respondent. He was also sent to the processing factory of the claimant to supervise the production of the garments directly. As an operations manager of the respondent, he should have the general power of an agent. The arbitration tribunal therefore held that the claimant indeed had reason to believe that Mr. Wu had confirmed the sample fabrics in his capacity as a representative of the respondent and that this confirmation was valid.
　　After signing the contract, the practice of the parties was to confirm the sample fabrics. The use of the confirmation card signed by Mr. Wu was evidence that the parties had followed a similar practice before: that the buyer would confirm the material and the color of .the fabrics for the outer layer of the garments before production. The line, `brown: as provided before' on the confirmation card meant that the brown fabric had been confirmed before in previous production and there was no need to reconfirm it this time. What is the legally binding effect of such a practice? There is no specific provision dealing with this in the Foreign Economic Contract Law of the People's Republic of China (the Foreign Economic Contract Law). But Article 5 of the Foreign Economic Contract Law provides that `International practices are applicable to any issues not provided for in the laws of the People's Republic of China.’ Clause 1 of Article 9 of the Convention on International Sale of Goods Contracts of the United Nations provides that any established practices agreed between contracting parties and any usual practices established between them shall be binding on both parties. The confirmation of the sample fabrics could be considered a usual practice established by the parties in their preceding transactions and should be binding upon them.
　　In their performance of the contract, the parties adopted transaction practices which involved both sampling and descriptions of the materials. A sample in this case was not a finished product in itself but part of the product, eg, the sample fabrics for the outer layer of the garments. The sample was provided by the seller and confirmed by the buyer: Therefore, the goods produced by the claimant according to these sample fabrics should not be disputed as far as the material for the outer layer of the garments was concerned. In its defence, the respondent denied that Mr. Wu was the manager of its garment department and also that the sample fabric confirmed by Mr. Wu was an authorised sample. It claimed that it had provided sample fabrics to the claimant and that the garments delivered by the claimant did not conform with these sample fabrics. Based on facts which were confirmed by investigation, the arbitration tribunal rejected the arguments of the respondent and held that the sample fabrics confirmed by Mr. Wu should be taken as the materials for production.
　　The recognition by the arbitration tribunal that the sample fabrics confirmed by Mr. Wu should be taken as the materials for production had the effect of resolving all subsequent issues. The respondent could no longer refuse to accept the goods on grounds of non-conformance with the sample fabrics, and the claimant had the right to claim damages from the respondent if the respondent refused to hire a ship or book necessary shipping space to take delivery of the goods.
 
(Zeng Yinyan)
