CASE 11

REASONABLE INSPECTION PERIOD
　　The dispute in this case concerns the quality of light bulbs supplied under a sale and purchase contract. A clause in the contract covers inspection of the quality of the goods, but does not specify a valid time period for inspection. Half a year after taking delivery of the goods, the buyer finds upon inspection that there is a problem with the quality of the goods. It is not until more than one year after taking delivery of the goods that the buyer sends the goods to the commodities inspection authority for examination. The buyer uses the commodities inspection results to support its claim for compensation. The arbitration tribunal holds that the reasonable length of time in which the buyer should have bad the goods inspected has already passed and dismisses its request for return of the goods.
 
THE ARBITRATION DECISION
 
I. Facts of the Case
　　 On 7 September 1989 ,the claimant and the respondent signed contract No. 89ED-102 in the city of Guangzhou . The contract stipulated that the respondent would supply to the claimant 186,000 ‘220V, 100W’ opal light bulbs at a unit price of US$0.113, FOB Foshan. The total price would be US$21,018. The goods would be shipped before 20 November 1989 . Payment would be settled by T/T within two days of shipment. A certificate of inspection issued by the China Commodities Inspection Bureau or a certificate issued by the seller would serve as the ultimate basis for assessment of the quality, quantity and weight of the goods.
　　After the contract was signed, the claimant took delivery of the goods on 20 November 1989　at Foshan Electrical Appliances and Lighting Company, the place of delivery specified by the respondent, and sent the goods to the warehouse at Shaxi in Guangzhoufor storage. On 30 November 1989, the claimant paid US$21,018 for all the goods by T/T. Upon inspecting the goods in early June 1990, the claimant found that the sockets of sampled light bulbs had turned white and were rusting. No conclusion was reached after an exchange of letters and telephone conversations between the parties. In February 1991, the claimant sent the goods to the Guangdong Commodities Inspection Bureau for inspection. On 3 April 1991, the technical service report issued by the Guangdong Commodities Inspection Bureau revealed that the appearance and quality of the goods did not fulfilll State standard GB 10681-89 requirements for `universal light bulbs'. On　 18 June 1991, the claimant applied to the Shenzhen Branch of the Commission for arbitration.
　　The claimant requested that the following rulings be made:
　　1. The respondent be held liable for breach of the contract and ordered to take back the goods under the contract.
　　2. The respondent return US$21,018 to the claimant, pay US$1,541 compensation for interest lost, US$735 compensation for warehouse rental plus interest, and US$1,303 compensation for lost profits.
　　3. The respondent bear all arbitration fees for the case.
　　
The main arguments of the claimant were as follows:
　 1. When the claimant received the notice from the respondent to take delivery of the goods on 20 November 1989 , the last ocean-going freight that would ensure punctual delivery of the goods had departed. Therefore the claimant sent the goods to the warehouse at Shaxi in Guangzhou　for temporary storage until the arrival of the next ship.
　　2. It was stipulated in the contract that the respondent would be responsible for the application for commodities inspection. Despite repeated requests by the claimant, the respondent never produced any valid commodities inspection documents. Therefore the certificate of inspection arranged for by the claimant should be used as the ultimate basis for assessment of the quality, quantity and weight of the goods.
　　3. According to state standards, the period of effective use for these goods was one year. It was unreasonable for the respondent to deny responsibility on the ground that ‘the effective period of use for the light bulb sockets was six months’.
　　4. The respondent delayed delivery of the documents for the goods. Ownership of the goods should therefore not be deemed to have been passed.

The main claims in the defence of the respondent were:
 　　1. Upon taking delivery of the goods, the claimant had already opened the boxes for inspection, and no quality problem regarding the appearance of the goods was found. Moreover, there were ten days between the day of delivery and the day of payment. The claimant should have had enough time to arrange for an inspection of any kind. However, it was not until more than half a year after the goods had been stored that the claimant notified the respondent of a problem with the appearance of the goods. According to international trade practice, the claimant had exercised its inspection rights in respect of the goods and accepted the goods under the contract. It was no longer entitled to raise an objection.
　　2. The claimant had continuously controlled the goods and stored them in a third party warehouse of which the conditions and environment were not known by the respondent. It was not until one-and-a-half years after delivery that the goods were inspected and a certificate of inspection was issued. The results of this inspection could not reflect the appearance and quality of the goods as they were upon delivery. Moreover, in 1989 [the time of the delivery], China had no statutory requirements for the inspection of light bulbs for export.

II. Views of the Arbitration Tribunal

The terms of the transaction stipulated in the contract in this case were FOB Foshan and the shipping period was agreed to be before 20 November 1989 . On 17 November 1989 , the respondent notified the claimant that the goods were ready for shipping. On 20 November 1989 , the claimant hired a truck to take delivery of the goods at the factory in Foshan which had produced the light bulbs. Upon taking delivery, the claimant had already opened the boxes and inspected the appearance of the goods, and found no problem. The goods were sent to the warehouse at Shaxi in Guangzhouon that day for storage. Ten days after taking delivery, the claimant paid the respondent for all the goods. Thereafter, the goods remained under the control of the claimant. It was more than one year and four months later when the claimant produced the technical service report issued by the Guangdong Commodities Inspection Bureau as the supporting document for its claim.
　　From these facts, it is clear that the claimant had inspected the appearance of the goods at the time of delivery and then removed the goods from Foshan, the place of delivery. Thereafter, the goods were under the control of the claimant. According to international trade practice, the buyer of any goods must inspect those goods within the shortest practicable period and should notify the seller of any non-conformity of the goods within a reasonable length of time after it has or should have noticed the non-conformity. The buyer in this case, the claimant, had had enough time to inspect the goods at the time of delivery and immediately after the time of delivery. However, it was not until one year and four months later that it produced supporting documents for this claim. The arbitration tribunal can not be satisfied that this was the shortest practicable period for inspection and a reasonable length of time in which to raise a claim. The claimant had therefore already lost its right to claim.

III. The Decision
　　Based on the above facts, analysis and assessment, the arbitration tribunal decided that:
　　1. The claimant's requests for return of the goods; refund of the contract price and payment of compensation for lost interest shall be dismissed.
　　2. The arbitration fees for the case shall be borne by the claimant.
This decision shall be final.

DISCUSSION
　　The dispute and the arbitration award for this case appear to be very simple; however, the subject of the dispute is typical of transactions for the sale and purchase of goods. The dispute involves an important issue: if the valid period of time for inspection by the buyer after taking delivery of the goods is not specified in the contract, how should a reasonable period for inspection be determined?
　According to international trade practice, after receiving goods delivered by the seller, the buyer has the right to inspect the goods. If the buyer does not exercise its right to inspect the goods the buyer cannot be deemed to have accepted the goods even if it has accepted the documents evidencing title to the goods or it has paid for the goods. This practice is endorsed by law in many countries. For example, Article 34 of the British Sale of Goods Act 1893, which greatly influenced legislation in other countries, stipulates that,
　　(1)    When goods are delivered to the buyer which the buyer has not previously examined, the buyer is not deemed to have accepted them unless and until it has had reasonable opportunity to examine them to ensure that they are consistent with the description in the contract.
　　(2)    Unless otherwise agreed, the seller should allow the buyer             reasonable opportunity to examine the goods at the time of delivery to ensure that the goods are consistent with the contract description.
　　However, the buyer's right to inspect is not unrestricted. The buyer cannot delay examination of the goods indefinitely. If the buyer has retained the goods for a reasonable length of time without notifying the seller of the non-conformity of the goods, the buyer is deemed to have accepted the goods (Article 35, Sale of Goods Act 1893). In some circumstances, the buyer has yet to examine the goods after taking delivery but has already resold or used the goods in production. This may be deemed by a court or arbitration tribunal to be against the seller's right of entitlement to the goods. The buyer is deemed to have accepted the goods and is no longer entitled to reject them.
　　The inspection authority for the goods was specified in the contract in this case, but the valid period for inspection was not. It was not until six months after taking delivery of the goods that the buyer raised an objection to the quality of the goods and a further eight months later that it requested that the inspection authority examine the goods. The certificate of inspection issued by the commodities inspection authority certified that the appearance of the goods (the light bulbs) did not meet state standards. The arbitration tribunal held that, according to international trade practice, the buyer of the goods must inspect the goods within the shortest practicable period of time and should inform the seller of any non-conformity within a reasonable length of time after it has noticed or should have noticed the non-conformity. At the time of delivery and after the time of delivery, the buyer in this case had had sufficient time to inspect the goods, but it was not until one year and four months later that the buyer produced a certificate of inspection certifying the non-conformity of the goods. A reasonable length of time for inspection had already lapsed.
　　It should be noted that the arbitration tribunal defined the ‘reasonable length of time’ for the inspection of goods by the buyer as ‘the shortest practicable period of time’. There are two major elements of this definition. First, the buyer must not delay inspection and should complete inspection as soon as possible after taking delivery. Second, the practicality of inspection should be considered. In other words, the `shortest period of time' must be determined by taking the actual circumstances into consideration. I am of the opinion that the actual circumstances mentioned here are closely related to the mode of delivery and the nature of the goods delivered. Under FOB terms, for example, the buyer may have to transport the goods after it has sent a vessel to take delivery of the goods at the port of loading. The distance to the final port of destination may be very great. Under such circumstances, the ‘shortest period of time’ should extend to after the goods have reached the port of destination. This is totally different from the situation under DES delivery terms. Under DES terms, the delivery of the goods takes place at the port of destination. The transportation of the goods before delivery is totally unrelated to the buyer. In addition, the length of time allowed for inspection varies greatly for different types of goods. Two or three days is already too long for perishable goods, but machinery, especially equipment that makes up part of a production line, needs a longer period of time. As the determination of a reasonable period of time for inspection is a difficult issue, the buyer and the seller often specify the period in the contract. If there is no such provision in the contract, one can only rely on the judge or the arbitrator's knowledge and commonsense.
　　The goods involved in this case were universal opal light bulbs. Upon taking delivery, the buyer did not notice any problem with the appearance of the goods. Half a year after taking delivery, however, the buyer noticed that the sockets of the bulbs had turned white and had started to rust. As this type of quality problem should be immediately apparent and given that the goods had already been stored in the warehouse by the buyer for half a year, one cannot help suspecting that the whitening and rusting of the sockets were caused by improper storage of the goods by the buyer. Moreover, after noticing the problem, the buyer did not send the goods to an inspection agent for examination until more than six months after taking delivery. This delay was also unacceptable to the tribunal. Therefore it was reasonable that the arbitration tribunal dismiss the request of the buyer.
