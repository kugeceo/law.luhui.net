CASE 4

STARTING POINT FOR PERIOD OF CLAIM
The buyer and the seller agree in the contract that the buyer may appoint a commodities inspection agent to reinspect the goods upon their arrival at the port of destination. The period of claim allowed to the buyer under the contract is 45 days from the date of arrival of the goods at the port of destination. The port of destination under the contract is a port in the
United States, but the name of the port is not specified. The invoice issued by the seller, however, specifies the name of the port of unloading. After a dispute concerning the quality of the goods arises, the question is raised as to whether the period of claim should start from the day on which the goods initially reach the country; albeit at another port, or from the day when the goods reach the port specified on the invoice.
 
THE ARBITRATION DECISION
 
I.    Facts of the Case
On 3 September 1990, the claimant (the buyer) authorised a trading company to sign a contract No. (90) GTSMC014, with the respondent (the seller). It was agreed in the contract that:
　　1.     The respondent would supply 285 tonnes of silicon to the claimant FOBST Huangpu, Guangzhou. The cost of the goods under the contract was to be US$730 per ton at a total cost of US$208,050.
　　2.     The shipping deadline was specified as the end of September 1990.
　　　3.     Payment was to be made by L/C.
　　4.     The goods were to be inspected by the commodity inspection authority of China .
　　When the goods arrived at the port of destination, the buyer could appoint SGS to reinspect the goods. If, upon reinspection, the quality and quantity of the goods were, found to be inconsistent with the terms of the contract, the buyer was entitled to claim damages- The period of claim allowed to the buyer was to be 45 days of the arrival of the, goods at the port of destination.
　　After signing the contract, the claimant issued an L/C on 11 September 1990 with the respondent as beneficiary. On 10 October 1990 , the respondent loaded 280 tonnes of silicon at the port of Huangpu in Guangzhou .During the period from 19 November 1990 to 26 November 1990 , the goods arrived at Birmingham , the port of destination, in batches and were put in storage. The claimant, pursuant to the inspection clause of the contract, appointed SGS to reinspect the 280 tonnes of silicon. On 21 December 1990 , SGS submitted to the claimant the preliminary inspection report on the 280 tonnes of silicon. On 27 December 1990 , a second report was submitted. The inspection revealed that the quality of the silicon was inconsistent with the terms of the contract. On 22 December 1990 , the trading company representing the claimant raised an objection to the respondent concerning the quality of the goods based on the results of the reinspection of SGS and expressed the wish of the claimant to settle this dispute through friendly negotiations. Thereafter, both parties discussed the matter several times, but came to no agreement. The claimant applied for arbitration on 19 April 1991 .

In its application for arbitration, the claimant submitted that:
　　1.     The respondent did not follow the provisions of the contract that specified delivery of the goods before 30 September 1990.
　　2.     As there were serious quality problems with the goods, the respondent had agreed to pay compensation of US$200 for every tonne of goods and entered into an agreement regarding this compensation, No. (91) GTCLA001, with the claimant on 16 January 1991. On I February 1991, the respondent suddenly negated the agreement on compensation pointing out that it was not the supplier of the goods. It also requested that the claimant provide the original lead seal on the containers.
　　3.     The claimant had requested that the respondent send to the United States persons to reinspect the goods, but the respondent had not heeded the request. This caused a direct loss of over US$80,000 to the claimant. The claimant requested that an award be granted against the respondent to take back the 280 tonnes of substandard silicon and to compensate the claimant for all economic losses. The respondent should also bear the arbitration fees.

The major points of defence of the respondent were as follows:
　　1.       The claimant could not sue on the contract. Although it was specified in the contract that the buyers were the claimant and a trading company, only the trading company signed the contract. This showed that the respondent did not have a contractual relationship with the claimant.
　　2.       The respondent had never reached any agreement on compensation with the ‘claimant’. The so-called agreement on payment by the respondent of US$200 for each tonne of goods as compensation was faked by a development company. Nor could the fax message sent by the staff of the import/export department of the respondent, to the effect that US$300 would be paid on each tonne of goods as compensation, represent the respondent because the respondent's company chop was not stamped on the message and the general manager of the respondent had not given authorisation.
　　3.       The claimant had never provided the original lead seals from 14 containers and there was not sufficient evidence to prove that the goods were exactly those delivered by the respondent.
　　4.       The claim made by the claimant was not valid. Clause 13 of the contract provided that the period of claim by the buyer was 45 days after the goods reached the port of destination. From the customs declaration documents submitted by the claimant, the goods reached the US port on 4 November 1990. By the time the respondent received SGS's initial report on 22 December 1990, the 45-day period of claim stipulated in the contract had already expired. It was not until 29 January 1991 that the respondent received SGS' formal report so the report had therefore missed the deadline for claim. Moreover, the container numbers in the report were different from those shown on the bill of lading, the original lead seals were not supplied, and the claim was not raised within the effective period of claim. Therefore, the respondent considered the claim raised by the claimant was not valid.

II.               Views of the Arbitration Tribunal
　　1.     It was specified in the contract No. (90) GTSMC014, that the buyer was the claimant and a trading company. Though the contract bore only the seal of the trading company and the signature of a manager of that company, the claimant submitted at the time of applying for arbitration a letter of authorisation issued by the claimant on 10 July 1990 authorising the trading company to be the representative of the claimant for signing a contract in the People's Republic of China for the purchase of silicon. The letter of authorisation was valid until 31 December 1990.
　　During the performance of the contract, the respondent had never queried the identity of the claimant as the buyer of the goods. In a fax message dated 4 February 1991 to the claimant, the respondent stated that, `As we have never had direct contact with each other on this matter, we did not understand each other's intention, and this caused some unnecessary misunderstanding. Hereafter, please contact us directly on this matter to avoid unnecessary misunderstanding that may be caused by intervening parties.' Thereafter, the claimant and the respondent communicated directly by fax to discuss the compensation under the contract.
　　Under such circumstances, the respondent could not claim that there was no direct trading relationship between the claimant and the respondent and that the claimant was not the buyer under the contract.
　　2.     The contract was an FOB contract. It was stipulated in the contract that the port of loading was the port of Huangpu of the People's Republic of China and that the port of destination was a port in the United States, but the name of the port was not specified. On the invoice issued by the respondent upon delivery, however, it was specified that the goods were to be shipped from Huangpu in China to Birmingham, Alabama in the United States. It was obvious that the respondent was well aware that the port of destination of the goods was Birmingham in the United States. The date on which all the goods under the contract arrived at Birmingham was 26 November 1990. According to the  respondent, the initial reinspection report of SGS was received on 22 December 1990, which was within the 45-day claim period. Therefore the claimant's claim was valid.
　　3.     Examination of the information provided showed that the numbers on 13 of the 14 containers agreed with the numbers quoted on the certificate of reinspection. Only the number of one of the containers, which was SEAU 1387840, was recorded on the certificate of reinspection as SEAU 13878740. The arbitration tribunal was of the view that this could be a typographical error. The respondent did not have sufficient grounds to infer from the inconsistent serial number of one container that all the goods reinspected were not the original shipment, and therefore, the arbitration tribunal did not support this claim. Further; the arbitration tribunal also noted that a staff member of the respondent had, in a fax message dated 15 January 1991 to a development company, expressed the intention to pay compensation to the claimant. In a letter to the claimant on 4 February 1991, the responsible officer of the respondent explicitly stated that ‘we should pay compensation in accordance with our contract and the formal inspection report issued by SGS on 27 December 1990.’ In conclusion, the arbitration tribunal considered that the claimant had the right to claim compensation with the support of the reinspection report. The compensation should be in the form of a discount on the payment of the goods actually delivered, taking into account the quality of the goods. After investigation and examination of the evidence submitted by the parties, the arbitration tribunal considered that the discount should be US$200 per metric tonne. The respondent should pay compensation to the claimant at this rate plus interest.

III.           The Decision
　　1.       The respondent shall compensate the claimant for its losses of US$56,000 (calculated at the rate of US$200 compensation for each metric tonne of silicon) plus interest of US$5,226.70 (calculated at the rate of 8 per cent per annum for the period from November 1990 to December 1991), amounting to a total of US$61,226:70. This amount shall be paid to the claimant within 30 days of the date of this award. Further interest shall be payable in case of late payment.
　　2.       The arbitration fees of the case shall be borne by the respondent.
　　This decision shall be final.

DISCUSSION
　　A major issue in this case is how the date of arrival of goods at the destination should be determined. This important issue in this case is crucial in the determination of whether the claimant's claim was made within the claim period stipulated in the contract. It was agreed in the contract that the period of claim was to be within 45 days of the arrival of the goods at the port of destination. However, the name of the port was not specified in the contract. The port of destination of the goods was specified, however, on the invoice issued by the respondent upon delivery of the goods, as Birmingham in the United States. It was at the request of the claimant that the respondent specified the port of destination as Birmingham; the respondent would not fix a destination itself. In other words, after signing the contract, the parties made a supplemental agreement specifying Birmingham as the port of destination. The place of business of one of the parties was in Guangzhou in China and that of the other party was in the United States. Both countries were signatories to the 1980 Convention on International Sale of Goods Contracts of the United Nations (the Convention) and the Convention was applicable to this case. According to Article 38(2) of the Convention, `If transportation of goods is involved, the inspection can be postponed to the time when the goods arrive at the destination.' As the claim for compensation can only be made after the goods have been inspected, the period of claim should naturally start from the time when the goods arrive at the destination. It was on 26 November 1990 that all the goods arrived at Birmingham, which was the destination. The reinspection report issued by SGS was dated 21 December 1990. The claimant made a claim for compensation on 22 December 1990 with the support of the reinspection report. The claim of the claimant was made 26 days after the arrival of the goods at the destination and was, therefore, within the period of claim provided for under the contract. It was inappropriate for the respondent to take the destination of the goods simply as the United States and to take the date of arrival of the goods in the United States as the starting date of the period of claim.
 
 
(Han Jian)
