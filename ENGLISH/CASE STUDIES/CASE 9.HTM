CASE　9

THE　PACKING　CLAUSE
 When negotiating and undertaking a sale of goods contract, much attention is often paid to principal terms, such as the quality and quantity of the goods and the payment clause, at the expense of other conditions which may appear to be unimportant, but which can also result in a breach of contract for which compensation must be paid.
The following case shows the importance of the packing clause in an international sale of goods contract.
 
THE ARBITRATION DECISION
 
I.                  Facts of the Case
 
On 26 March 1991, the claimant (the buyer), a company in China, and the respondent (the seller), a company in the United States, signed a sale of goods contract, No.91WMT-911139US, together with a supplemental agreement. Pursuant to the contract, the respondent agreed to sell to the claimant 2,183.616 tonnes of free size kraft paper at a price of US$353 per tonne, totalling US$770,816.45, C&F Shanghai, payable by L/C payable on sight. To facilitate identification at the time of taking delivery, the claimant later sent three fax messages to the respondent on 27 March 1991, 8 April 1991 and 9 May 1991 requesting that the respondent add the words ‘Gram’, ‘Weight’ and ‘Width’ to the packing label ‘91WMT-911139US/Shanghai, China’, as agreed in the contract and to specify these details on every roll of goods. In its replies dated 8 May 1991 and 10 May 1991, the respondent expressed acceptance of the above requests. On commercial invoice No. 91075-366, and the copy of ocean bill of lading delivered to the claimant, the respondent indicated that the above label appeared on every roll of kraft paper.
On 5 April 1991, the claimant, through the Changsha branch of the Bank of Communications, issued the L/C, with the respondent as beneficiary. On 16 May 1991, the claimant paid the cost of the goods in the amount of US$770,816.45. The goods reached Shanghai on 19 June 1991. The Hunan Province Through-Transport Company, which was commissioned to take delivery of the goods, noted that the labels on the goods were not printed as agreed in the contract. At the request of the respondent, the claimant took delivery of the goods. Many similar goods were transported to the port of Shanghai on the same vessel. With the exception of a limited portion of these goods, which bore distinctive marks, the majority of the goods were not identified by labels. Officers at the port distributed the goods according to the quantity of the goods specified on the bill of lading presented by each person taking delivery.
When the goods were shipped to the Yueyang go down of the Hunan Province Supply and Marketing Transport Company, the claimant noticed that there were 1,248 rolls of kraft paper as agreed, but the total weight was short by 292.857 tonnes. The claimant immediately notified the respondent of the situation and took photographs of the goods as requested by the respondent. Inspection of the goods was later carried out by the Hunan Commodities Inspection Bureau. It was noted in the certificate of inspection No. 91A078 that;
Inspection of the goods upon arrival shows that none of the rolled kraft paper bears on its surface the label agreed upon in the contract. The weighing of 63 of these rolls, which represents five per cent of the whole batch of goods, with a calibrated balance, reveals that the actual weight of each roll is the same as the weight specified on the label. By adding up the weight of the 1,248 rolls of kraft paper as specified on the labels, the total weight equalled 1,890.759 tonnes, which was 292.857 tonnes short of the total weight agreed upon in the contract.
In October 1991, the ultimate supplier of the goods explained to the respondent in a letter, ‘As you required the goods urgently, we arranged for the immediate delivery of the goods. The goods were to leave the port of departure on 10 April 1991, and two days (8 April to 10 April) were not sufficient time for the manufacturer to print the labels on the goods.’ To resolve the problem of the shortage of goods, the claimant, the respondent and the supplier went to the Yueyang go down of the Hunan Province Supply and Marketing Transport Company on 17 December 1991 and 18 December 1991 to reinspect the goods (by checking the bills and weighing the goods). The results of the reinspection confirmed there was a shortage of goods equal to 292.857 tonnes as claimed by the claimant.
On 27 November 1991, before the reinspection, the claimant sent a letter to the Shanghai Branch Office of China Ocean-Going Vessels Agency Company, the carrier of the goods, to discuss the issue of the shortage of 292.857 tonnes -of kraft paper. The Shanghai Branch Office of China Ocean-Going Vessels Agency Company forwarded the letter to Guangzhou Ocean Transport Company, the actual carrier of the goods, who replied on 24 February 1992 as follows:
The shortage of the goods was due to the unclear marking of the goods themselves. Clause 5 of the terms of the bill of lading specified that, ‘before loading, the goods should be properly packed and correctly and clearly labelled by the shipper. The name of the port of destination should be clearly marked on the outside of the goods in characters of at least five cm in width.
These labels should be clear and legible when the buyer takes delivery. All fines and costs incurred by the inadequate or improper packing and marking of the goods shall be borne by the shipper.' We are therefore not responsible for any shortage due to the unclear marking of the goods.
Thereafter, the parties entered into repeated negotiations, but no consensus was reached. On 8 May 1993, the claimant made a written application for arbitration to the Shenzhen Branch of the Commission.
In its application, the claimant submitted that the respondent did not mark the goods as agreed, resulting in the shortage of 292.857 tonnes of goods. It requested that:
 
1. the respondent compensate the claimant for the loss of US$103,378.52 caused by the absence of the labels;
2, the respondent compensate the claimant for economic losses in the amount of Rmb721, 039.45 suffered by the claimant as a result of the inability to sell the goods due to the shortage of the goods;
3. the respondent bear the arbitration fees and expenses incurred by the claimant in the handling of this case;
4. the case be examined as soon as possible and the claimant be allowed to resell the stored goods immediately in order to avoid further losses, as the goods in dispute had been held in storage for quite a long period of time.
 
The respondent's major points of defence were as follows:
 
1. The respondent was only a middleman in the transaction. It was not directly involved in the shipment of the goods and only passed on messages as required by the supplier and the purchaser. The shortage of goods should be the responsibility of and compensated for by the supplier. The respondent would do its best to help the claimant claim the amount, and return the total amount of compensation due, to the claimant.
2. The respondent insisted that the amount of indirect losses in the claim, which totalled over Rmb700, 000, was unacceptable to the respondent because losses of this nature could only be assessed based on the shortage in the goods rather than on the entire amount of goods ordered. The goods not delivered would not incur storage costs. It was also unreasonable to assess indirect losses on a time period of more than 20 months. The respondent had never objected to the claimant selling the other goods and had never prevented the claimant from doing so. As regards the price and the time of the sale of the other goods, this was the claimant's own business and had nothing to do with the respondent. The time basis for assessment of any indirect losses could at most be extended to December 1991, when the three parties inspected the goods and confirmed the shortage.
3. The claimant should bear part of the responsibility for the acceptance, inspection, transshipment; storage and reinspection of the goods.
 
II.               Views of the Arbitration Tribunal
 
1. From the provisions of the contract and the fax messages between the parties after the contract was signed, it can be concluded that the parties had reached an agreement on the contents of the shipping mark for the goods. However, the respondent did not comply with the labeling requirements. Evidence showed that there were over 8,000 tonnes of unmarked free size kraft paper on the vessel ‘Jinjiang’ hired by the respondent. The goods delivered by the respondent comprised a part of those unmarked goods. As the goods were not marked as agreed, the goods delivered were confused with similar goods shipped by other owners, making it impossible for the claimant to take delivery of the goods it had ordered according to the pre-agreed label. The officers at the port in Shanghai distributed the goods according to the number of units of goods as specified on the bills of lading of the various owners of the goods. As a result, the goods actually collected by the claimant were short by 292.857 tonnes. This was confirmed by the certificate of inspection No. 91A078, and by reinspection by the claimant, the respondent and the supplier on 18 December 1991 at Yueyang. It could therefore be concluded that the absence of the agreed label on the goods resulted in the shortage in the goods received by the claimant. Based on this, the arbitration tribunal held that the respondent, as the seller, should be responsible for the shortage and should return to the claimant the payment made on the unreceived amount of goods, plus interest. As requested by the claimant in its application for arbitration, the payment on the unreceived amount of goods would be made in US dollars and the interest would be payable in Renminbi. In its calculations, the claimant used an exchange rate between Renminbi and US dollars of 5.54:1 and an interest rate of 6.45 per cent per month. The tribunal considered this appropriate. The interest should be calculated from 16 May 1991, when the claimant paid the contracted cost of the goods, up to the date of the arbitration award.
In its defence, the respondent claimed that it was a middleman in the transaction and that the shortage in the goods should be the responsibility of the supplier who should pay the compensation. However, the respondent obviously had no grounds for this claim in its defence. The parties to the contract were the claimant and the respondent and the contract also specified that it was the respondent who should prepare the required labels on the goods. As the pre-agreed labels were not affixed to the goods, the responsibility could only rest on the respondent. The failure on the part of the supplier, as the actual shipper, to prepare the labels was a matter between the supplier and the respondent. Only the respondent can make a claim against the supplier, and the claimant had no part in this.
2. Evidence also showed that the claimant had immediately notified the respondent of the absence of the labels and the shortage in the goods, and had taken photographs of the goods as requested by the respondent. The claimant had arranged for the goods to be inspected by the Hunan Commodities Inspection Bureau and had been actively involved in the reinspection of the goods at Yueyang on 17 December 1991 and 18 December 1991 with the respondent and the supplier. The claimant had fulfilled its obligations by doing this. The arbitration tribunal held that before the reinspection by the three parties, the respondent did not ask the claimant to sell the goods. In order to carry out reinspection to confirm the shortage of goods, the claimant had to keep the goods in their original state when delivered and could not, therefore, sell the goods. The storage of the goods was a consequence of the default on the part of the respondent. Therefore the storage costs incurred before the reinspection by the claimant, the respondent and the supplier on 18 December 1991 and before confirmation of the shortage in the goods (at the rate of RmbO.15 each tonne per day from 19 June 1991, when the goods arrived at the port of Shanghai, to 18 December 1991, the date of the confirmation) should be borne by the respondent. The interest on the value of the goods which could not be sold as a result of this should also be borne by the respondent. (The value of this part of the goods was US$667,437.93, which was equivalent to Rmb3, 697,606 at the US$/Renminbi exchange rate of 1:5.54. Interest should be calculated from 16 May 1991, when the contract price for the goods was paid, to 18 December 1991, when the shortage was confirmed by the three parties, at the Renminbi interest rate of 6.45 per cent per month.) The interest expenses and storage costs incurred after the confirmation of the shortage in the goods could not be proved to have been caused by the respondent and should be borne by the claimant itself.
3. The respondent should bear all the arbitration and handling fees. As no evidence was produced to support the other expenses incurred by the claimant in the handling of this case, the claim for compensation from the respondent of these costs was not entertained.
 
III, The Decision
 
Based on the above analysis and judgment, the arbitration tribunal ruled as follows:
1. The respondent shall pay to the claimant the amount of US$103,378.52 on the unreceived 292.857 tonnes of kraft paper; plus interest of Rmb113, 777.
2. The respondent shall compensate the claimant for Rmb51,050 being the storage costs for the goods and shall compensate the claimant for a loss of interest of Rmb168,537 caused by the storage of the goods.
3. The arbitration fees shall be borne by the respondent.
This decision shall be final:
 
DISCUSSION
 
The dispute involved in this case is common in international trade. After the contract has been signed, the seller issues the L/C and the seller ships the goods. A shortage in the goods is discovered upon arrival of the goods and the buyer suffers losses as a result. There can be many reasons for shortages in delivered goods. In this case, the shortage was caused by the failure on the part of the seller to affix the pre-agreed shipping labels. This highlights the importance of the packing clause in international sale of goods contracts.
The parties entered into a contract for the sale and purchase of free size kraft paper. To facilitate identification at the time of taking delivery of the goods, the claimant later sent three fax messages to the respondent requesting the respondent add the words ‘Gram’, ‘Weight’ and ‘Width’ to the packing label ‘91WMT-911 I39US/Shanghai, China’ agreed to in the contract and to specify these details on every roll of kraft paper. The respondent accepted all of the above. On the basis of this, the arbitration tribunal concluded that the parties had reached a new agreement regarding the shipping labels for the goods.
Upon the arrival of the goods at Shanghai, the port of destination, however, the claimant noticed that the labels on the goods were not printed as previously agreed. Afterwards, the ultimate supplier explained in a letter to the respondent that the manufacturer did not have enough time to print the shipping labels on the goods because the goods were required urgently. In the case of most sales and purchases of goods, the non-conformance of marks with the description in the contract would not affect the receipt, quality and quantity of the goods. But it cannot be concluded from this that it is not important to follow the requirements regarding labels as specified in the contract. In some cases or under certain circumstances, failure to print labels according to the requirements of the contract could have serious consequences. This case is a perfect example of this. Many goods of similar descriptions were shipped to Shanghai on the same vessel. With the exception of a limited part of these goods, which bore distinctive marks, the large part of the goods were not identified by labels. Officers at the port distributed the goods according to the amounts of the goods as specified on the bills of lading presented by every person taking delivery. Consequently, though the number of units of goods distributed to the claimant was the same as that appearing on the bill of lading, the total weight was several hundred tonnes short.
Who should be responsible for the shortage? Before applying for arbitration, the claimant asked for compensation from the carrier. In international trade, ocean shipping is a principal element in the performance of a contract. As a result of the distances involved in shipping goods or of natural: disasters, shortages and even loss of goods often arise during transportation. If losses are the responsibility of the carrier, it should pay compensation. However, the carrier in this case did not accept the claim made by the claimant because the shortage was due to the unclear marking of the goods. The terms of the bill of lading specified that before loading, the goods should be properly packed and correctly and clearly labelled by the shipper. The name of the port of destination should be clearly marked on the outside of the goods in characters of at least five cm in width. The labels were to be clear and legible when the buyer takes delivery. All fines and costs incurred by the inadequate or improper packing and marking of the goods would be borne by the shipper. The carrier was of the opinion, therefore, that it was not responsible for the shortage due to the unclear marking of the goods. Judging from the facts of the case, the carrier had sufficient grounds for this explanation..
Under such circumstances, the claimant could only apply for arbitration pursuant to the arbitration clause of the contract. The decision of the arbitration tribunal was based upon the following facts:
1.     The parties had reached an agreement regarding the shipping label of the goods, but the respondent did not print the labels on the goods as agreed. The arbitration held that by failing to affix the pre-agreed shipping labels, the respondent had breached the provisions of the contract for the packing labels for the goods.
2.     There was a shortage in the goods received by the claimant. The reason for the shortage was that the goods were confused with similar goods belonging to other owners on the same vessel. The reason for the confusion was that similar goods on the vessel did not bear any marks, making it impossible for the owners of the goods to take delivery by identifying the goods by their labels. Officers at the port could only distribute the goods according to the number of units specified on the bills of lading produced by the owners of the goods. As a result, the goods actually obtained by the claimant were several hundred tonnes short of the weight of the goods it had ordered. The arbitration tribunal concluded that the respondent's failure to print the labels as agreed to under the contract caused the shortage in the goods. Therefore, the respondent was liable to pay damages.
 
The arbitration tribunal reasoned as follows: the party that breaches a contract should bear the consequences of the breach; as the respondent, in failing to print the labels as agreed, was in breach of the contract, the respondent should bear the consequences of the breach; there existed a causal relationship between the breach of the respondent and the losses of the claimant; therefore, the respondent should compensate the claimant for its losses. It should be acknowledged that in normal circumstances, the absence of labels on goods would not necessarily result in a shortage of goods. From the facts of this case, however, it was clear that the absence of labels made it impossible for the claimant to identify its goods. As there were similar goods also without labels and belonging to various owners on the same vessel, it was impossible to identify which goods were covered by a particular bill of lading. Officers of the port had no choice but to distribute the goods according to the number of items specified on the bills of lading. As the actual weight of each roll of kraft paper differs, it was natural that some owners would end up with more than they had ordered and others with less. Therefore, the absence of labels in this case was the cause for the shortage in the goods collected by the claimant. The arbitration tribunal had sufficient reason to conclude that the respondent should bear the responsibility for the shortage of goods.
The respondent was a middleman in the transaction. The goods were shipped directly by its supplier and the job of printing the labels was also commissioned to the supplier. As a result of the negligence of its supplier, similar goods on the same vessel were confused due to the absence of the labels. The supplier of the respondent should ultimately be responsible for the matter. However, the rights and obligations under the sale of goods contract were limited to the buyer and the seller. The respondent should first admit its responsibility before it claimed damages under its contract with the supplier. The respondent's argument that the supplier should be responsible for the breach was therefore weak and could not hold.
If the respondent had printed the labels as agreed and there was still a shortage in the goods, the shortage could be a result of an insufficient quantity of goods shipped by the respondent or a result of the carrier's having mislaid the goods. If the respondent could prove that it had delivered the correct quantity of goods to the carrier, the carrier could not blame the shortage on the absence of clear labels. Unless there were exemption clauses in the contract for such an event, the carrier would have been responsible for the shortage in the goods. Under such circumstances, the respondent would not be the only person who could be held responsible for the shortage, and the claimant could not hold the respondent responsible. The printing of the labels was therefore the key issue in the determination of responsibility of the parties in this case.
In international trade, with the exception of some goods which do not need to be packed or are not worth packing, most goods need a certain amount of packing to ensure quality and correct quantities during distribution. Trading parties should make specific provisions in their contracts for the packing of goods which need packing. Major elements in a packing clause would include: packing material, manner of packing, packing costs and shipping labels. In this case, as the claimant had emphasised the packing clause and made detailed provision in the contract for the requirements of the shipping labels, it had sufficient grounds to claim damages from the respondent when the respondent breached this clause and caused the shortage in the goods.
In international sale of goods contracts, the packing clause has as much importance as clauses regarding the quality, specifications, quantity, price and payment terms of the goods. Parties to a sale of goods contract should not only lay down detailed provisions for this, taking actual circumstances into consideration, but should also ensure accurate performance of each of the clauses. The emphasis on some provisions and neglect of others can, as this case has shown, cause unnecessary losses.
 
(Xie Weimin)
