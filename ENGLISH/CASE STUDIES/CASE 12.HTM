CASE 12

DOES CHANGE IN STATE POLICY CONSTITUTE
FORCE MAJEURE?
 It is agreed in the contract that the buyer will issue an L/C before a specific date, but subsequently, the buyer cannot issue the L/C within the agreed period. The buyer claims that the State has adjusted its policy on foreign exchange control, making it impossible for the buyer to draw on its foreign exchange account. Is this force majeure? Is the seller entitled to claim compensation?
 
THE ARBITRATION DECISION
 
I.       Facts of the Case
　　On 7 January 1988, the claimant and the respondent signed a contract No. 88QH - 407B 85CK for the purchase of 225 tonnes of high-density low-pressure polyethylene by the respondent from the claimant at a total price of US$292,275. The buyer was to issue an irrevocable L/C payable on sight on 12 January 1988 for the contracted amount.
　　The claimant prepared the goods for shipping, booked the shipping space and prepared for the delivery of the goods. However, the respondent did not issue the L/C within the time stipulated in the contract, and on 13 January 1988 asked the claimant to change the payment terms to D/P (documents against payment). The claimant found this unacceptable. On 18 January 1988, the respondent informed the claimant that it would issue an L/C on 19 January 1988. The claimant accepted this and promised to sell the respondent the goods that were to arrive on 27 January 1988 in order to fulfilll its obligations under the contract. However, the respondent failed to issue the L/C as promised. On 25 January 1988, the claimant declared its termination of the contract and claimed damages from the respondent. The claimant stored the goods, which arrived on 27 January 1988, in the common godown. Thereafter, the claimant sold the goods by batches in Hong Kong.
　　The claimant requested that the respondent compensate the claimant for losses. After fruitless negotiations, the claimant applied for arbitration on 21 June 1988, stating:
　　1. The claimant claimed that the failure on the part of the respondent to issue the L/C within the period stipulated in the contract was a fundamental breach of the contract. The respondent should be held liable and should compensate the claimant for all losses suffered as a result of this breach.
　　2. The claimant requested that the respondent compensate the claimant for a total of HK$241,893.91 representing eight items where losses were incurred, including traveling expenses, communication expenses and price difference.
　　The claimant presented to the arbitration tribunal the invoices and confirmations from its bank that related to the sale of the goods in Hong Kong and the invoices for storage fees.Regarding the first issue raised by the claimant, the respondent claimed that the respondent was not able to open an L/C because the State had adjusted its foreign exchange control policy and the respondent could not draw on its foreign exchange account. This was a matter of force majeure and was not the fault of the respondent.
　　The respondent submitted supporting evidence to this effect to the arbitration tribunal. The respondent queried some of the eight items for which the claimant had issued its claim. The respondent was of the opinion that travelling expenses and communication expenses were normal business expenses of the claimant and that it was unreasonable for the claimant to request that the respondent bear these losses.
　　The respondent submitted that the sales invoices and bank confirmations produced by the claimant did not prove the price at which the goods under the contract were sold and could not be submitted as evidence as they did not reflect the claimant's actual losses.

II.  Views of the Arbitration Tribunal
　　After examining the application for arbitration, the defence and other documents submitted and listening to the presentations of the parties, the arbitration tribunal held that:
　　1. The documents submitted by the respondent could not prove that State policy at the relevant time directly prevented the respondent from performing its obligation under the contract to issue an L/C. The attribution to force majeure by the respondent did not hold and the respondent should be held responsible for the breach.
　　2. On the basis of the sales invoices and bank confirmations produced by the claimant, the total loss that the claimant claimed it incurred due to the price difference of the batch of goods when sold equalled HK$124,052.19. The arbitration tribunal did not consider that the sale invoices and bank confirmations presented by the claimant were sufficient to determine the actual price at which the goods were sold. However, having taken into consideration the falling market price of the goods, international trade practice and the business environment of Hong Kong, the invoiced amount, which reflected a price difference of 5.5 per cent from the original contract, was reasonable. Therefore the arbitration tribunal considered that the claim could be awarded.
　　3.  The method by which the claimant calculated the storage fees and the amount of storage fees that the claimant arrived at were not entirely reasonable. The claimant sold the goods under contract in six batches. The storage fees should be calculated according to the actual amount of goods that remained in the godown.
　　The total should therefore be HK$12,746.67.
　　4.  The interest on the amount owed under the contract should be calculated at the rate of seven per cent per annum from 11  February 1988 onwards until the actual date of sale of each batch of goods. Calculated on this basis the interest owed according to contract should total HK$14,837.12. The claimant's claim for bank interest, however, did not have sufficient documentary evidence to support it and should therefore be dismissed.
　　5.  The travelling expenses, telephone and fax expenses, towing fees; company expenses and berth age claimed by the claimant were the claimant's own costs.These claims were not justified and should be dismissed.

III.The Decision
　　1. The respondent shall compensate the claimant for HK$151,635.98 (including losses incurred due to price difference, storage fees and interest on the contract price of the goods) plus interest of HK$17,593.93 calculated at seven per cent per annum for the period from 18 April 1988 to 13 December 1989, amounting to a total of HK$169,230.
　　2. The respondent shall pay the above amount to the claimant within 30 days of the date of this award. Interest shall be calculated until the actual date of payment on any overdue amount.
　　3. The arbitration fees for this case shall be borne by the respondent.
This decision shall be final.

DISCUSSION
　　The key question in this case is whether the claim of force majeure raised by the respondent can be upheld. The claimant held that the respondent's failure to issue an L/C within the time period specified in the contract constituted a fundamental breach of contract and that the respondent should be liable for this breach. The respondent argued that it could not issue an L/C because the State had adjusted its foreign exchange control policies, making it impossible for the respondent to draw on its foreign exchange. The respondent considered this to be force majeure for which the respondent should not be held liable.
　　Force majeure refers to the failure to perform a contract or to perform a contract on time, not due to any fault or negligence of any of the parties to the contract but due to unforeseen events that could not have been anticipated or prevented by the parties. Under these circumstances, the parties are free of their obligation to perform the contract or can delay performance of the contract.
　　Force majeure is usually included as a clause in a contract. The laws of individual countries and some international conventions have certain provisions on force majeure and have established it as a legal principle. The laws and rules of different countries may vary in their exact definition of the term force majeure. Different terms are also used to express this concept. In common law, for example, has the term ‘voidance of contract’ and relevant rules that support this, whereas, continental law has provisions for a ‘change in circumstances’ or ‘nullity of contract’. Whatever term is used, the idea is similar. The major conditions of force majeure include an unexpected event that must occur after the contract was signed; an unexpected event that is the cause of any fault or negligence of the parties themselves; an unexpected event that cannot be anticipated by the parties at the time of the signing of the contract and cannot be prevented.
　　Clause 12 of the contract in this case on force majeure provided that in the event of unexpected events under circumstances generally accepted as force majeure which would prevent the delivery of the goods in accordance with the contract, the seller was to immediately inform the buyer by cable and, within 14 days, send to the buyer by airmail documents issued by the government or chamber of commerce of the place where the events occurred certifying the occurrence of these events. Without acknowledgment of this from the buyer, the seller would not be exempted from its responsibility by reason of force majeure. If the events of force majeure continued to exist making delivery of the goods impossible within one month after the dates of the delivery period agreed in the contract. had passed, the buyer would be entitled to cancel the contract, and the parties would not claim damages from each other. The failure on the part of the seller to obtain an export permit would not be deemed as an event of force majeure.
　　Article 24 of the Foreign Economic Contracts Law of the People's Republic of China (the Foreign Economic Contract Law) stipulates that, ‘events of force majeure refer to events which cannot be anticipated by the parties at the time of signing a contract and of which the occurrence and consequences cannot be avoided or overcome by the parties.’ Article 25 of the Foreign Economic Contract Law also provides that, ‘When one of the parties cannot perform all or part of its obligations under the contract by reason of force majeure, it should notify the other party in time in order to mitigate possible losses to the other party and should within a reasonable period of time provide evidence to this effect issued by the relevant authorities.’
　　In this case, if the foreign exchange really had been frozen as a result of changes in or the adjustment of government financial policies, this situation would be deemed an event which could not have been anticipated by the parties at the time of signing the contract and whose occurrence and consequences could not have been avoided or overcome by the parties. If the affected party had, upon realising that its foreign exchange was frozen by the government and that it could therefore not issue an L/C, immediately notified the other party and produced evidence to the other party within the time period stipulated in the contract, it would have been entitled to postpone the deadline for performing the contract. Upon the expiry of that period, if the event of force majeure continued to exist, either party to the contract would be entitled to cancel the contract. The party which was not able to perform the contract by reason of force majeure could be exempted from all or part of its obligations as the case may be.
　　In this case, however, after it notified the claimant by cable on 14 January 1988 that its foreign exchange was frozen by the State and that it could not issue an L/C before 12 January 1988, the time stipulated in the contract, the respondent failed to provide government documents proving the freezing of foreign exchange within 14 days as provided in the contract. Neither had it provided such proof within a reasonable length of time nor requested postponement of performance of the contract pursuant to the Foreign Economic Contract Law. It was not until the claimant submitted the case for arbitration that the respondent produced the documents proving the freezing of foreign exchange in 1988 to the arbitration tribunal as supporting documentation proving force majeure. Nor did the respondent notify the claimant when foreign exchange accounts were released and issue the L/C at that point in order to perform the contract. In these circumstances, it must be concluded that the respondent had not fulfilled its duty to make reasonable efforts to perform the contract. In the event of force majeure in international trade, in order for the party to be exempted from its obligation to continue performance of the contract, that party must have made reasonable efforts to perform the contract.
　　Another issue which must be mentioned is why the respondent had not presented the government document regarding the freezing of foreign exchange accounts to the claimant in time. The document was an internal government document and as such it was inappropriate to disclose it. This is understandable because China was still in the process of adopting planned economy policies at the time in question. Even considering this, however, it could still have been possible for the respondent to request a document explaining the situation from the relevant authorities. The respondent did not do this. As a result, it was not granted exemption from its obligations under the contract by reason of force majeure as it had requested.
