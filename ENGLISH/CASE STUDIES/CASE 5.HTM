CASE　7

CONTRACT UNCLEAR AS TO
TECHNICAL STANDARDS FOR GOODS
 
 
When the technical standard of the equipment is not clearly specified in the contract, how should the standard be set? How to determine whether the claim for damages is appropriate?
 
THE ARBITRATION DECISION
 

I.Facts of the Case

 
On 10 November 1987, the claimant, a food and beverage company and an economic trading centre in Shenzhen, issued a letter of authorisation authorising the respondent to import a complete set of equipment for production using tin cans. On 23 November 1987, the claimant (as the buyer and Party A) and the respondent (as the seller and Party B) signed a  ‘contract for the import of a production line for the packaging of canned drinks’. It was agreed in the contract that:
 
1.     The majority of the machines in the production line was to have been produced in or after 1977 (the filling machine was to be manufactured in 1971 and renewed in 1977). It should be a complete set of equipment. There should be no serious damage to the appearance of the equipment. Production volume should reach 450 cans per minute.
2.     The value of the equipment totalled US$1,096,800. The claimant was to pay a deposit of US$200,000 upon signing the contract. When installation of the production line started, the claimant would, upon receiving written notice from the respondent, pay US$600,000 by T/T. US$100,000 would be paid upon the arrival of the installation engineer at Shenzhen. The balance would be paid within ten days after the equipment started normal operation. The equipment was to arrive in China no later than 31 January 1988.
3.     Upon the arrival of the goods at Shenzhen, the parties would together arrange initial inspection by the commodities inspection bureau for the quantity, specifications, model number and appearance of the goods. After trial production, the equipment would be inspected a second time. The inspection would check whether the equipment could reach the production speed of 450 cans or more per minute.
4.     The warranty and compensation for the goods would be handled in accordance with clause 4 of the contract regarding the quality requirements for the equipment. The major components of the production line, including the sealing machine, filling machine, packing machine and testing machine were warranted for an operating life of ten years on the condition that there were no external factors affecting their operation and that good maintenance and repair were carried out. If there were any quality problems with these machines, found to be intrinsic to the machines themselves within three years, Party B would repair or replace the machines. If the equipment as a whole was not operating well or could not reach the production volume of 450 cans per minute, Party A (ie the claimant) could, within 90 days and with the support of the certificate of inspection issued by the China Commodities Inspection Bureau, claim from the seller five to 30 per cent of the price for the goods under the contract according to the actual deviation in the operating speed. If the machine did not run, 100 per cent of the price of the goods under the contract could be claimed but Party B would be allowed to disassemble and take back the machines.
5.     Details of the major pieces of equipment under the contract (including quantity, model number, machine number, manufacturer and year of production) were listed in an attachment.
 
After the contract was signed, the major equipment arrived at Shenzhen within the period of time stipulated in the contract. The claimant paid to the respondent a total of US$900,000. On 24 January 1989, a certificate of inspection on the appearance and quantity of the goods was issued by the commodities inspection authority.
Installation and testing of the equipment was completed on 23 March 1989. On 24, 25 and 26 March 1989, members of the inspection authority, together with representatives of the buyer and the seller, conducted an on-the-spot inspection of the machines in operation. A printed certificate, No. 001594, was issued on 5 August 1989 by the inspection authority regarding the quality of the production line.
Afterwards, the claimant claimed compensation from the respondent supported by this certificate of inspection. The parties were unable to resolve their disputes through negotiation and in March 1990; the claimant submitted the case to arbitration.
 
In its application for arbitration, the claimant requested that:
 
1.     the respondent pay compensation of US$270,000 (US$900,000 x 30 per cent) for the poor operation of the machines which did not reach the technical standards stipulated in the contract;
2.     the respondent compensate the claimant for economic losses of US$90,000 caused by the insufficient quantity of the equipment and poor quality of part of the equipment;
3.     the respondent bear the costs of replacing certain parts bearing different model numbers which had to be replaced;
4.     the respondent compensate the claimant for a direct loss of Rmb700,000 which occurred during the installation and testing of the equipment;
5.     the claimant be released from its obligation to pay the balance of US$200,000 as a result of the failure of the respondent to fulfill its obligation to maintain and repair the equipment; and
6.     the arbitration fees for the case be borne by the respondent.
 
During the hearing on 9 November 1991, the claimant withdrew item 3 of its claim.
 
The major grounds for claim submitted by the claimant were as follows:
 
1.       Inspection of the trial run showed that the average speed of production of the equipment was 350 cans per minute which was substantially lower than the 450 cans per minute stipulated in the contract. The default rate of the goods was as high as 13.5 per cent. The major reasons for the poor operation of the equipment were that some of the machines were too old, some were inappropriate and there were insufficient spare parts.
2.       The contract provided that the majority of the machines should have been manufactured after 1977. Among the machines provided, however, eight machines had no mark showing their date of manufacture. Examination by the commodities inspection bureau showed that the carbon dioxide storage tank, which did not function, was produced in 1961.
3.       The respondent did not provide the following equipment listed in the contract:
a stainless steel heat converter;
a set of automatic cleaning and sterilising equipment (ancillary to the filling machine);
a forcing pump;
a set of installation and repairing tools; and
a mixer.
4.     It was stipulated in the contract that the service and handling charges amounted to US$120,000 and the cost of parts and maintenance totalled US$96,000. It was agreed in the contract that the respondent was to provide maintenance for one year and replacement of parts for three years after the equipment came into normal operation, but the respondent had never fulfilled its obligation to repair the equipment. There was also no evidence that the respondent would fulfill this obligation in the coming three years. Nor had the respondent provided any spare parts. The claimant should therefore be released from its obligation to pay the balance of the price of the goods in the amount of US$200,000.
5.     As the machines were too old and the equipment was not running well, almost 900,000 cans were wasted during the seven-month testing period. The cost of each can was Rmb0.80. The claimant therefore suffered a direct economic loss of Rmb700, 000.
 
The respondent presented the following defence and counterclaim:
 
1.       On 10 November 1987, the claimant issued a letter of authorisation to the respondent, Shenye Electronics Company, giving it `full authority to act as agent to import a complete set of equipment for production using tin cans. Shenye Company is authorised to inspect the equipment and make purchases once the basic terms for the goods as specified in the contract are met.' The relationship between the respondent and the claimant was therefore that between principal and agent rather than between buyer and seller. As the licence approached expiry, the respondent imported the equipment pursuant to the authorisation and requirements of the claimant within the agreed time period. The respondent had thus fulfilled its obligations under the contract.
2.       The production volume of the equipment was able to reach 460 cans per minute. The standard provided for in the contract was not based on the average speed. It was inappropriate for the claimant to claim damages on the grounds that the average production volume could not reach 450 cans per minute. Further, the respondent had never guaranteed the volume of production and the passing rate. It was also inappropriate for the claimant to claim damages on the grounds that the default rate of the goods was high. The poor operation of the equipment during the commodities inspection was due to two reasons. First, the basic conditions for production were not fulfilled: the workers were not skilled, the syrup was not properly mixed, the quality of the cans was not good, and the cans could not fit the machines properly. Second, the claimant delayed completion of the plant. The machines stood in the open for six months before installation, which naturally affected the performance of the equipment. The failure of the operation of the equipment to reach the claimant's expectations was related to poor maintenance of the equipment by the claimant.
3.       The claim that eight items of equipment were not marked with the date of manufacture was fabricated. Every major machine was marked with the date of manufacture and the manufacturer also provided documents on the machine numbers. It was unreasonable for the claimant to say that the carbon dioxide storage tank was produced in 1961 to negate the post-1977 production date of all the other major equipment of the production line in order to claim full compensation.
4.       It was agreed in the contract that the goods would undergo initial inspection for quantity, specifications, model number and appearance upon arrival at Shenzhen. Upon the arrival of the goods at Shenzhen, the claimant inspected the large part of the goods. The goods arrived at Shenzhen at the end of January 1988, but the certificate of inspection on the appearance and quantity of the goods was not issued until January 1989, which was one year after the arrival of the goods. Save for a mixer which was missing for reasons that were unknown, the items mentioned in the certificate as missing were in fact provided, with the claimant only insisting that they were missing.
5.       It was not true that the respondent did not fulfill its obligation to provide maintenance. The claimant was responsible for this itself, first because the claimant delayed payment of more than US$200,000 and second, because after the equipment was handed over to the claimant for use on 26 March 1989, the respondent had arranged for an American engineer to stay in Hong Kong for three months in the event servicing was needed. Third, the respondent had never received a formal notice from the claimant requesting maintenance.
6.       During 1988 and 1989, a large number of the cans leaked because there were quality problems with the imported can themselves. All manufacturers in China using this type of aluminum can could prove this. The damage to the cans was not caused by the equipment. Further, some of the empty cans used in the trial run were produced in Foshan and did not fit the trial run requirements for the necessary tin cans as stipulated in the contract. The claimant had no grounds for claiming an economic loss of Rmb700,000.
7.       The total contract price of the goods was US$1,096,800. During installation, the respondent purchased accessories at a cost of over US$30,000 and incurred further rental costs of US$5,000. By the time of the arbitration, the claimant had only paid US$900,000. An amount of over US$200,000 was still outstanding.
 
In its supplementary submission dated 28 August 1990, the respondent amended its defence and raised the following counterclaims:
 
1.       The claimant should pay the outstanding amount of US$196,800 due on the equipment, together with interest accruing from 3 April 1989.
2.       The claimant should compensate the respondent the sum of US$30,000 being payment for accessories not covered by the contract, and US$5;000, being rental costs plus interest accruing from 3 April 1989.
3.       The claimant should compensate the respondent the sum of US$20,000, plus interest thereon, being extra expenses incurred in the arrangement for installation personnel as a result of the failure on the part of the claimant to provide the plant and other conveniences as scheduled.
4.       The claimant should compensate the respondent for the salary and expenses paid to the American engineer who waited in Hong Kong for three months, totaling about US$40,000, plus interest thereon.
5.       The claimant should bear the expenses incurred from arbitration.
6.       The claimant's requests should be dismissed.
 
In its supplementary submission dated 19 June 1990 and during the hearing, the claimant raised the following counter-defences:
 
1.     Clause 12 of the contract stipulated that the respondent was to arrange for the production line to be designed within 30 days after signing the contract. However, for over two months after the conclusion of the contract, the respondent did not start the design of the production line. Nor did it provide installation drawings and descriptions for the design. By the time the equipment arrived, it was found that the original plant of the claimant was not suitable for use. The claimant had no choice but to build a new plant on another site. Therefore, the fact that the equipment remained in the open was due to the default of the respondent. Moreover, the equipment's remaining in the open for three months would not cause poor performance of the equipment. This was because the machines were packed in solid wooden boxes fit for long-distance ocean transport, and the boxes were lined with plastic film.
2.     Clause 2 of the contract provided that `the speed of production should reach 450 cans or more per minute'. As shown in the several drafts of the contract made by the respondent, 450 cans per minute should be the average speed. In the first draft, a minimum speed of 400-500 cans per minute was specified. The draft dated 8 November showed that the speed was 450 cans per minute. Therefore it was inappropriate for the respondent to interpret the speed of 450 cans per minute as being the speed of production to be attained within, and sustained for, a short period of time.
3.     The claimant considered the respondent's claim that it supplied accessories that were not included in the contract to be incomprehensible. It requested that the respondent produce evidence and explain the nature of the supply of the accessories, ie whether they were a gift or purchased with the authorisation of the claimant.
 

II. Views of the Arbitration Tribunal

 
Based on the evidence submitted by the parties and its own investigations, the arbitration tribunal made the following analysis and judgment:
 
1.       During the process of sourcing equipment for the tin can production line, a letter of authorisation was signed by the claimant, an economic trading centre and a food and beverage company. However, the `Contract for the import of a production line for the packaging of canned drinks' concluded later between the claimant and the respondent was a sale and purchase contract. All facts that arose during the performance of the contract proved that the relationship between the claimant and the respondent was that between a buyer and a seller and not that between a principal and an agent.
2.       All the clauses of the contract regarding the quality of the equipment (clauses 2, 4, 13 and 15) explicitly stated that the major technical requirement for the equipment was that the speed of production should reach 450 cans or more per minute. There was no other technical data. The certificate of inspection issued on 5 August 1989 by the Shenzhen Import and Export Commodity Inspection Bureau showed that after the goods arrived at Shenzhen, installation and testing was completed on 23 March 1989. On 24, 25 and 26 March 1989, the members of the commodities inspection authority and the representatives of the buyer and the seller conducted a thorough on-the-spot inspection of the machines. The results of the three-day inspection showed that the average speed of the equipment was 327-357 cans per minute. The conclusion was that the equipment could maintain a speed of 460 cans per minute for a short period of time, but could not maintain this high speed for an extended length of time because of deficiencies in, and other errors concerning, the equipment. Thus, the average speed of the line could not reach 450 cans or above per minute. The arbitration tribunal considered that the certificate of inspection showed that the equipment supplied by the respondent could not reach the standard of 450 cans or above per minute stipulated in the contract. Also, there was no evidence that the failure of the equipment to reach the standard under the contract was caused by damage to the machines and there were no external factors that could affect the performance of the equipment. The arbitration tribunal therefore supported the claimant's claim for damages pursuant to the contract. The poor running of the equipment and the difference between the actual speed of the equipment and the standard stipulated in the contract made it appropriate for the claimant to claim compensation of US$270,000 from the respondent.
3.       It was stated in the certificate of inspection issued on 24 January 1989 by the Shenzhen Import and Export Commodity Inspection Bureau that the following parts were missing: a stainless steel heat converter, a forcing pump, a set of automatic cleaning and sterilising equipment, a mixer and a set of installation and repairing tools. After verification, it was confirmed that a set of installation and repairing tools had been received. The claimant claimed economic losses of US$90,000 from the respondent resulting from incomplete delivery of the goods and the poor quality of part of the equipment, but did not produce evidence to prove this loss. Therefore, this claim was not supported.
4.       It was agreed in the contract that the major pieces of machinery to be supplied were to be manufactured in or after 1977. However, the carbon dioxide storage tank was manufactured in 1961 and did not fulfill contract requirements for the goods.
At the hearing, the respondent expressed its willingness to compensate for this. The submission of the claimant showed that a new carbon dioxide storage tank would cost US$70,000, and the claimant requested compensation of US$40,000.The arbitration tribunal considered that though the carbon dioxide storage tank supplied by the respondent was manufactured in 1961, the claimant had used it. The respondent should therefore pay to the claimant US$20,000 to compensate for its losses.
5.       Regarding the request of the claimant that the respondent pay Rmb700, 000 compensation for the waste of almost 900,000 tin cans during the trial run due to the dilapidated condition of and poor co-ordination among the machines, the arbitration tribunal considered that:
a)       The contract in this case was for the sale and purchase of machines. It was stipulated in the quality clause of the contract that if the equipment did not operate properly or could not reach a certain production volume, Party A (ie, the buyer) was entitled to claim compensation of five to 30 per cent of the contract price of the goods from the seller on the strength of the certificate of inspection. Pursuant to this clause, the claimant had claimed compensation of
b)      US$270,000 for the poor quality of the machines, and should not make further claims regarding quality problems.
c)       b. It is provided in law that the party suffering losses has the obligation to prevent further losses, or relinquish its right to claim compensation on these losses. The claimant was well aware that the failure rate of the product was very high, but it did not take appropriate measures when it knew that there was a problem with the quality of the equipment, to prevent further losses. Instead, it continued production, which wasted almost 900,000 tin cans. The claimant is not completely absolved of responsibility for this. Therefore, the claimant's claim for direct losses could not be supported.
6.       Investigation provided no evidence to prove that the claimant had issued a notice to the respondent requesting the latter to provide repair services. Therefore the request of the claimant for the respondent to pay US$200,000, being the balance of the contract price of the goods, on the grounds that the respondent had not provided repair services, could not be supported.
7.       The claimant had already received the equipment delivered by the respondent, which had been installed, tested and could go into operation. In accordance with the contract, the respondent's request for the claimant to pay the outstanding contract price of the goods of US$196,800 plus interest should be granted. The interest was to be calculated from 3 April 1989 to 16 April 1992 at the annual rate of seven per cent. This amounted to US$41,864. Together with the outstanding contract price of the goods of US$196,800, the total sum payable amounted to US$238,664.
8.       There was no evidence to prove that the expenses for an American engineer who waited in Hong Kong for three months were incurred at the request of the claimant. Therefore, this claim of the respondent could not be upheld.
9.       Regarding the two counterclaims of the respondent for the cost of accessories not included in the contract and the expenses for the installation personnel, the arbitration tribunal held that there was insufficient evidence to support this claim and that the requests could therefore not be granted.
10.   The arbitration and handling fees of the case should be borne 40 per cent by the claimant and 60 per cent by the respondent.
 
III. The Decision
 
Based on the above facts, analysis and judgment, the arbitration tribunal decided as follows:
1.       The claimant's claim for an economic loss ofUS$90,000 caused by the incomplete delivery and the substandard quality of part of the goods shall be dismissed.
2.       The claimant's claim for compensation from the respondent for a direct loss of Rmb700,000 incurred during the installation and testing of the equipment shall be dismissed.
3.       The claimant's request that its obligation to pay US$200,000, being the balance of the contract price of the goods, be released on the grounds that the respondent has not fulfilled its obligations to provide repair services shall be refused.
4.       The respondent's counterclaim for US$30,000 being the cost of accessories not included in the contract and for US$5,000 being rental costs plus interest from 3 April 1989 shall be dismissed.
5.       The respondent's counterclaim for US$20,000 being extra expenses for the arrangement of installation personnel incurred by the failure on the part of the claimant to provide the plant and necessary conditions as scheduled, plus interest thereon, shall be dismissed.
6.       The respondent's counterclaim for about US$40,000 being salary and expenses for an American engineer who waited for three months in Hong Kong, plus interest thereon, shall be dismissed.
7.       The respondent shall pay US$290,000 to the claimant (ie, US$270,000 compensation and US$20,000 loss on the carbon dioxide storage tank). The claimant shall pay to the respondent the balance of the contract price of the goods plus interest, a total of US$238,664. After setting off the above amounts payable, the respondent should finally pay to the claimant US$51,336. This amount shall be paid by the respondent to the claimant within 30 days of the date of this award. Interest shall be payable in the event of late payment.
8.       The arbitration fees of the case shall be borne as to 40 per cent by the claimant and 60 per cent by the respondent.
 
This decision shall be final.
 
 
DISCUSSION
 
Under an agency arrangement, the agent represents the principal when signing a contract with a third party or carrying out other legal acts pursuant to the instructions of, and with the authority given by, the principal. The rights and obligations under the contract and arising out of these actions are binding on the principal. In the case of sale and purchase contracts, when the agent, pursuant to the instructions of the principal, .signs a sale and purchase contract on behalf of the principal with a third party, the contract will become a contract between the principal and the third party and the principal and the third party will be responsible for performing the contract.
The agent is not a party to the contract and will not be personally liable for the performance of the contract. Although there was a letter of authorisation signed between the parties in this case, the dispute arose neither from the letter of authorisation nor from the contract concluded between the third party and the respondent as agent but from the `Contract for the import of a production line for the packaging of canned drinks' concluded by the claimant (as the buyer) and the respondent (as the seller). The contract included some basic clauses indispensable in sale and purchase contracts such as clauses defining the contracted items, the price for the goods, payment terms and commodities inspection. The transaction entailed the import of the equipment by the respondent and the reselling of this equipment to the claimant. The dispute over the contract should be regarded as a dispute arising from a sale and purchase contract.
The second issue in this case is how to determine the appropriate technical standard for the equipment and allocate responsibilities accordingly when the contract does not clearly specify this technical standard. The required speed of production was one of the issues of dispute in this case. The speed provided for in the contract was `450 cans or more per minute'. The claimant claimed that the equipment did not reach this production speed whereas the respondent claimed that the contract did not specify whether the required speed was the maximum speed or the minimum speed, and that therefore the speed of 450 cans per minute should be the highest speed which could be reached and sustained for a short period of time. After analysing the situation, the arbitration tribunal dismissed the claimant's claim. The speed of production should be the speed that could be reached and maintained by equipment operating under normal conditions. If the speed of production could reach a certain level, but at this speed the equipment would sustain damage or would stop soon after reaching it, this is obviously not the mode of operation that can be reasonably anticipated by a reasonable man. This speed can only be regarded as a limit and not as a standard for operation under normal conditions.
When signing contracts involving technical standards, technical requirements should be specified in the contract as clearly and comprehensively as possible; to avoid misunderstanding or dispute.

Article 22 of the Foreign Economic Contract Law of the People's Republic of China stipulates that, `When one of the parties suffers losses as a result of breach of contract by the other party, it should adopt timely and appropriate measures to prevent further losses. If it does not take appropriate measures in time to mitigate its losses, it shall not be entitled to claim compensation for such further losses.' The mitigation of losses is a rule generally accepted in commercial law in different countries and in international treaties. Where there were defects in the equipment, the claimant in this case did not take appropriate action but allowed the equipment to go into operation, resulting in the waste of 900,000 tin cans and the loss of Rmb700, 000. The arbitration tribunal did not support the claimant's claim for compensation for this loss. If the claimant had attempted to make the defective equipment reach the technical standard stipulated in the contract and had taken appropriate measures to improve the quality of the equipment, the expenses incurred by the claimant to improve the quality of the equipment should then be borne by the respondent who supplied the defective equipment. The claimant would have been entitled to claim for those expenses and this claim would be more likely to obtain the support of the arbitration tribunal. The decision on the case would probably have been totally different if the claimant had taken appropriate measures to mitigate its losses.
