CASE 6

RIGHT TO REJECT DEFECTIVE GOODS

When a buyer finds goods delivered by a seller to be defective, how should it exercise its right to reject the goods? How should the goods be handled after a buyer has effectively rejected them?

THE ARBITRATION DECISION

I.                  Facts of the Case

On 13 March 1990, the claimant (the buyer) and the respondent (the seller) signed a contract, No. JHC9004. It was agreed in the contract that the respondent would sell to the claimant 1,200 kg of L-Cysteine HCL Monohydrate USP 21. The goods were to be delivered before 30 April 1990. The price was US$16 per kg, CIF Acapulco Gross, Mexico. The total cost of the goods under the contract was US$19,200. The goods were to be packed in iron barrels each weighing 25 kg. Payment would be made by irrevocable L/C payable on sight.

In clause 6 of the contract, which provided for the issues of warranty and claims for compensation, the seller guaranteed that the goods to be supplied would be made with the best materials by the best craftsmanship. The goods would be new and would not have been previously used. The quality and specifications of the goods would fulfill exactly the requirements stated in the contract. The warranty period for the goods would be 12 months from the date the goods arrived at the port of destination.

Upon arrival at the port of destination, the goods would be reinspected at the local office of the China Commodities Inspection Bureau. If the quality or quantity of the goods was found to be inconsistent with the requirements of the contract, the buyer would be entitled to claim compensation from the seller by making a claim in writing, within 90 days from the date the goods were unloaded, and by producing the certificate of inspection issued by the China Commodities Inspection Bureau (excluding areas covered by the insurance company or the carrier). In the event a claim was made, the seller would replace without charge the goods which did not fulfilll the requirements of the contract, make up any missing goods or reduce the amount payable on the goods. All associated expenses, including the transportation costs when taking delivery, loading costs, storage costs and the buyer's inspection costs, plus interest, should be borne by the seller and payable to the buyer immediately in cash. If the inspection could not be completed within the specified period, the buyer would be entitled to extend the period of claim on condition that the seller was notified of this in advance.

After signing of the contract, the respondent delivered the goods, which totalled 1,200 kg, within the stipulated period. When the goods arrived in Mexico as agreed under the contract, the client of the claimant, upon examination, discovered a problem with the quality of the goods and asked the claimant to take back the goods and bear the freight costs. On 27 July 1990; the claimant sent a telex to the respondent requesting that it resolve the problem. On 30 July 1990, the respondent replied to the claimant saying that the quality problem may have arisen because of the damp conditions during the long period of ocean transportation.

The respondent also stated that if the claimant insisted upon returning the goods, the respondent would only bear the freight costs from Shenzhen to Mexico. On 6 September 1990; however; the respondent stated that it was willing to take back the goods. The agreed port was Shenzhen, China. The consignee was Shenzhen Xinhua Import and Export Trading Company. The party to send the notice to the consignee of the arrival of the goods was the respondent. In its reply, the respondent emphasised that it would not be responsible for the freight costs. On 14 September 1990, in a telex to the claimant, the respondent repeated that it agreed to take back the goods and stated that it would share the freight costs from Mexico to Shenzhen with the claimant. It would refine the goods and rearrange delivery.

The Mexican client of the claimant sent back the goods which arrived at HongKong in December 1990. The claimant said that when the bill of lading was sent to the consignee specified by the respondent, no one took delivery of the goods. The goods were left at Hong Kong until the time of arbitration. The claimant requested that the respondent refund the payment made for the goods, but the respondent never did so. On 6 June 1990, the claimant applied for arbitration at the Shenzhen Branch of the Commission. In the application and subsequent supplementary materials, the claimant requested that:

1.     the respondent refund the payment of US$19,200 on the goods;

2.     the respondent pay US$5,597 interest on the cost of the goods;

3.     the respondent pay the freight costs for the return of the goods, a total of cost US$1,750;

4.     the respondent pay the storage costs of US$12,618 for the returned goods (costsincurred after 1 May 1993 would be calculated separately);

The claimant submitted that after the returned goods arrived at Hong Kong in December 1990, the carrier sent the bill of lading to the party specified by the respondent as the consignee, but no one took delivery of the goods. The respondent later advised that the designated party was no longer the consignee. The storage costs had been paid by the claimant until the time of arbitration. The respondent should return the payment on the goods and compensate the claimant for its losses.

The respondent submitted that after it designated the port of destination and consignee for the returned goods on 6 September 1990, the consignee did not receive any documents from the claimant. Moreover, the claimant did not ship the goods to Shenzhen as agreed, but left the goods in Hong Kong until the time of arbitration. The respondent could not issue an L/C before having received the returned goods, so therefore could not make payment. The respondent only learned that the consignee had been dismissed when the claimant went to Shenzhen to enquire into the matter.

This was the result of force majeure. The respondent was not to be held responsible in any way for failure to take back the goods and would not accept the claimant's claim for compensation.

The claimant said that the goods were not sent to Shenzhen because the customs declaration procedures could not be completed. The goods could only go to Shenzhen after the consignee designated by the claimant completed the customs declaration formalities. Therefore, the claimant was not responsible for the failure to send the goods to Shenzhen.

II.               Views of the Arbitration Tribunal

After the respondent sent the goods to Mexico in accordance with the contract, the claimant's client, IFF, found, upon inspection, that the quality of the goods did not meet its requirements. On the basis of this inspection, the claimant asked the respondent to take back the goods. Pursuant to the contract, the seller should take back, without charge, goods which do not conform with requirements for the goods under the contract. But the contract also provided that the buyer should base its request on a certificate of inspection issued by the China Commodities Inspection Bureau. Investigations showed, however, that there was no PRC inspection authority at the port of destination in Mexico. According to international trade practice, the buyer should have then asked an independent third party and the seller to carry out the reinspection. In this case, IFF was the claimant's client; it was therefore inappropriate for the claimant to request the return of the goods on the basis of the certificate of inspection issued by IFF. However, the parties had already agreed that the goods could be returned. A dispute arose regarding responsibility for the payment of the freight costs. The claimant requested that the freight costs be paid upon arrival of the goods, whereas the respondent wanted the freight costs to be prepaid. The respondent later expressed in a telex, dated 14 September 1990, that it ¡®[agreed] to take back the goods. We will share with you the freight costs for the goods from Mexico to Shenzhen.¡¯

The evidence submitted showed that the goods sent back by the claimant reached Hong Kong on 24 January 1991. Upon the goods' arrival, Votainer United Services Company Limited (Hong Kong), shipping agent for IFF, sent notification of the arrival of the goods to Shenzhen Xinhua Import and Export Trading Company, the consignee designated by the respondent. As no one came to take delivery of the goods, Votainer sent a letter to the claimant on 25 February 1991 to inform it of the situation. The claimant then notified the respondent on 26 February 1991 that the returned goods had been sent to Hong Kong.

Evidence also showed that the respondent had stated clearly in its telex to the claimant on 6 September 1990 that the port of destination of the returned goods should be Shenzhen and the consignee was to be Xinhua Import and Export Trading Company. The party to be notified of the arrival of the goods should have been the respondent. However, the port of unloading specified on the bill of lading for the returned goods dated 13 December 1990 was Hong Kong instead of Shenzhen and the party to notify of the arrival of the goods was Xinhua Import and Export Trading Company instead of the respondent. The column destination of transshipment was not filled in.

The arbitration tribunal held that:

1. The claimant should bear the major part of the responsibility for the failure to accept the returned goods, and storage of the goods in Hong Kong until the time of arbitration. The reasons given were as follows:

a)       The claimant did not follow the instructions of the respondent regarding the port of destination and the party responsible for notifying the consignee of the arrival of the returned goods and sent the goods to Hong Kong. When the goods arrived at Hong Kong, the claimant did not notify the respondent in time.

b)      When the goods arrived at Hong Kong, the claimant could have and should have redirected the goods to Shenzhen, the port of destination specified by the respondent. However, the claimant did not take steps to redirect the goods. If the goods had arrived at Shenzhen and the respondent had been notified of this, the customs declaration of the goods would have been the responsibility of the respondent. But the claimant did not do this and, therefore, could not claim that the goods could not be sent to Shenzhen because customs declaration formalities could not be completed.

c)       Articles 86(1) and 88(2) of the Convention on International Sale of Goods Contracts of the United Nations (the Convention) provide that if the goods are perishable, or if the preservation of the goods involves unreasonable expenses, the buyer, who has the obligation to preserve the goods, must take necessary steps to sell the goods and, as far as possible, notify the other party of its intention to do so. In this case, it was already almost three years before the goods arrived at Hong Kong on 24 January 1991. The supporting documents submitted by the claimant showed that as at 1 May 1993, the storage costs incurred for the goods in Hong Kong totalled US$12,618. If the charges incurred after 1 May 1993 were also taken into account, the storage costs would be US$17,000, which approximates the payment made for the goods. It can be seen that as a result of the failure on the part of the claimant to take necessary steps to sell the goods in this almost three-year period, the storage costs incurred had reached an unreasonable level. Also, the goods, having been stored in Hong Kong for such a long time, may have deteriorated, resulting in losses which could and should have been avoided or mitigated against.

Therefore, the arbitration tribunal does not support the claimant's claim that the respondent return all payment made for the goods with interest and bear all storage costs.

2. The respondent should bear part of the responsibility for its failure to accept the goods on time; after the respondent learned from the claimant that the goods had arrived at Hong Kong and that no one took delivery of the goods, it did not follow up the matter with the claimant. After the respondent and the claimant had confirmed that the designated consignee, Xinhua Import and Export Trading Company, was no longer the consignee, the respondent took no action to remedy the situation. The respondent's claim that it be relieved of responsibility because the dismissal of the consignee was the result of force majeure could not be supported.

3. The dispute over the contract originated with the quality of the goods. Though the certificate of inspection produced by the claimant was issued by IFF, its Mexican client, the respondent had clearly agreed to the claimant's request for the return of the goods. Therefore, the respondent acknowledged that there was a quality problem with the goods.

4. Though the claimant should be largely responsible for the failure to return the goods, the freight costs for the return of the goods, US$1,750, should be shared by the parties. In consideration of the fact that the respondent had agreed to the return of the goods, the respondent should bear part of the responsibility for the failure to return the goods.

The analysis in the first paragraph above showed that the return of the goods was not an issue in this case. However, the ensuing paragraphs showed that the respondent should pay an appropriate amount to the claimant to compensate it for the losses caused by the original quality problem with the goods and the failure to return the goods.

The arbitration tribunal considered that the freight costs of US$1,750 from Mexico to Hong Kong should be borne as to US$875 by the claimant and US$875 by the respondent. The respondent should pay compensation in the amount of US$3,000 to the claimant. The goods should be disposed of by the claimant.

III.           The Decision

1. The claimant's request that the respondent return the entire payment of US$19,200 made for the goods, plus interest of US$5,597 shall be dismissed.

2. The respondent shall, within 45 days of the date of this award, pay the claimant US$875 being part of the freight costs for the returned goods and compensation of US$3,000. Interest shall be payable where payment is late.

3. The goods stored in Hong Kong shall be disposed of by the claimant.

The arbitration and handling fees of the case shall be borne 90 per cent by the claimant and ten per cent by the respondent.

This decision shall be final.

DISCUSSION

The parties in the case had not chosen the governing law for the contract. According to the Foreign Economic Contract Law of the People's Republic of China (the Foreign Economic Contract Law), the applicable law should be ¡®determined by the doctrine of the most significant relationship¡¯. Under normal circumstances, the applicable laws for international sale of goods contracts should be the laws of the place of business of the seller at the time of signing the contract. As the place of business of the seller in this case was China, Chinese law should apply. Any matter not provided for by the laws of the People's Republic of China should be governed by international practice. When international treaties that are concluded by the People's Republic of China, or to which the People's Republic of China is a party, contain different provisions from the laws of the People's Republic of China, the international treaties should apply.

In this case, after the respondent had sent the goods to the port of destination, the claimant, upon inspection of the goods, considered that the quality of the goods did not conform to the contract description and requested replacement. By requesting replacement, the buyer was exercising its right to reject the goods. There is no specific provision in the Foreign Economic Contract Law applying to the rejection of goods under sale of goods contracts. However, Article 18 of the Foreign Economic Contract Law stipulates that failure on the part of one of the parties to perform the contract or to perform its obligations in the agreed manner constitutes a breach of contract and the other party is entitled. to claim damages or seek other reasonable remedies. The remedies referred to here should include exercising the right to reject the goods which do not conform to the contractual requirements for the goods.

The term `reject the goods' is not specifically used in the Convention, but the provisions of the Convention vest in the buyer the right to reject goods. In international trade, the delivery of goods is not simply a matter between the buyer and the seller and usually involves a third party, the carrier. The buyer usually takes delivery of the goods sent by the seller through the carrier, but reserves the right to reject the goods. When the goods are found upon inspection to be inconsistent with the contract, the buyer should try to preserve the goods and seek remedies, including rejection of the goods, requesting replacement of the goods and claiming damages.

The Uniform Commercial Code of the United States provides that if the goods do not conform to the contractual requirements for the goods or if the delivery is not made in the manner as agreed, the buyer is entitled to reject the goods. The Sale of Goods Act of the United Kingdom (the Sale of Goods Act) distinguishes between conditions and warranties in a contract. If the delivery of the seller breaches any condition to a contract, the buyer is entitled to reject the goods. If the seller's delivery breaches only a warranty, on the other hand, the buyer can only claim damages and is not entitled to reject the goods or treat the contract as void.'

Conditions referred to in the Sale of Goods Act include the following: the goods must be fit for the buyer's purpose; the goods must be of merchantable quality; the goods must correspond with the description agreed to in the contract; and the goods must correspond with the sample. However, there is no such explicit distinction in British case law. Whether there exists the right to reject is determined by the seriousness of the breach.2

The buyer must decide within a reasonable period of time after the goods are delivered whether to reject the goods, or it will lose this right. Both British and American laws have made provision for this. It is explicitly stipulated in the Convention that the buyer must, within a reasonable period of time after learning of the non-conformity of the goods with the contract, notify the seller of this non-conformity, specifying the nature and details of the non-conformity, or it will lose the right to claim compensation for the non-conformity of the goods with the contract.3

In this case, the claimant notified the respondent of the non-conformity of the goods and requested replacement of the goods within the time period stipulated in the contract. The respondent also agreed to replace the goods. Therefore, the claimant effectively rejected the goods.

Though the respondent agreed to inspect the goods and confirmed the port of destination and consignee for the returned goods, it had not come to an agreement with the claimant about the freight costs, the time of delivery of the replacement goods or the cost of the replacement goods. These actions by the respondent breached the provision in the contract that the replacement of goods should be made without charge. Under such circumstances, since the respondent, as the seller, failed to redeliver the goods within a reasonable period of time, this gave the claimant the relief that would have been available to it if the seller had not delivered the goods. Replacement of goods had therefore become return of goods, The problem is that, during the return of the goods, the claimant, by mistake, failed to return the goods to the respondent and controlled the goods instead. It did not endeavour to return the goods to the respondent as instructed by the respondent; nor did it seek further instructions from the respondent on whether it should dispose of the goods. The claimant therefore lost the relief that would have been available to it if the seller had not delivered the goods.

Taking into consideration the fact that the respondent also made mistakes in the process of returning the goods, it is reasonable for the arbitration tribunal to decide on an equitable solution, that the respondent should pay a certain amount of compensation to the claimant and bear part of the freight costs for the return of the goods.

The conclusion we can draw from this case is as follows: when the buyer notices that goods are defective, it must, in order to be able to exercise its right to reject the goods, notify the seller and seek the seller's instructions on the disposal of the goods within a reasonable period of time. After obtaining reasonable instructions from the seller regarding disposal of the goods, the buyer must follow these instructions exactly. Any consequences arising from its failure to follow the seller's instructions exactly, due to mistakes on its own part, will result in the buyer having to bear responsibility. In such a situation, it will suffer losses that could and should have been avoided.
