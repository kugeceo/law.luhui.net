CASE　8

IS PARTIAL DELIVERY A BREACH WHEN

SHIPPING PERIOD EXTENDED?

 
 When a shipping period is extended as a result of an amendment to an L/C, does partial delivery on the part of the seller constitute a breach of contract?
 
THE ARBITRATION DECISION
 

I. Facts of the Case

 
On 8 December 1988, a Sino-foreign joint venture (the buyer) and a Hong Kong company (the seller) signed a contract in China to import a plywood production line with ancillary facilities. The goods under the contract included a plywood production line comprising 69 machines, a plant of 7,650 square metres made of steel framing and aluminium alloy plates, transportation tools and office equipment. The cost of the goods under the contract was US$4,999,981.38, including freight charges. The terms of payment stipulated in the contract were as follows:
 
1.     Sixty per cent of the price of the goods under the contract, ie, US$2,999,988.83 was to be paid by the buyer by irrevocable, transferable L/C issued through the Bank of China to a Hong Kong company which would then issue an L/C payable on sight in favour of the seller.
2.     The seller, bearing the documents specified in the contract, would negotiate 80 per cent of the payment under the L/C.
3.     The remaining 20 per cent of the payment under the L/C was to be negotiated on the strength of a certificate issued by the buyer after the production line had been assembled and standard tested according to the contract.
4.     Forty per cent of the contract price, ie, US$1,999,992.55, was to be paid directly to the respondent by the Hong Kong shareholder of the buyer in the form of a deposit.
 
Delivery in batches was allowed under the contract. The deadlines for shipment were 10 March 1989 and 30 April 1989 respectively. It was stipulated in the contract that the seller should complete the assembly, testing and trial production of the production line within 150 days of the goods reaching their destination. Output from the trial production should reach 30,000 cubic metres per annum and should meet international quality standards. The contract also stipulated that the seller was to provide technical information, training skills and management personnel.
On 18 December 1988, the buyer issued, through the Bank of China, four irrevocable, transferable and divisible L/Cs in favour of the seller for a total amount of US$2,550,000 for the purchase of the 69 machines under the contract.
During the period from the issue of the L/C to 18 April 1989, the buyer amended the four L/Cs several times at the request of the seller.
By 29 June 1989, the buyer had received 15 machines delivered by the seller. The seller had received US$1,145,425.60.
After the arrival of the above machines at the port of destination, the China Commodities Inspection Bureau (the CCIB) inspected the goods. The results of the certificate of inspection showed that the number of pieces of equipment delivered by the seller fell short of the required number under the contract due to an omission at the time of dispatch. The equipment was old equipment produced in the 1960s, had been used for-years before delivery and was seriously rusted. It was also noted on the certificate that technical performance of the equipment could only be ascertained upon testing after installation.
The buyer and the seller discussed the matter, but reached no agreement. The buyer thus applied for arbitration of the dispute.
The buyer argued that it was agreed in the contract that the seller should deliver to the buyer a production line with a capacity of 30,000 cubic metres of plywood per annum that was to comprise 69 machines, but that the respondent delivered only 15 machines which were very old and with no technical information. The actions of the seller had seriously affected the economic benefits expected to be gained by the buyer at the time of concluding the contract and the buyer submitted that this was a fundamental breach of the contract.
 
The buyer submitted the following claims:
 
1.The seller should refund the buyer its payment of US$1,145,425.60 and compensate the buyer for the bank interest thereon at the monthly rate of one per cent for the period from 7 July 1989 to the date of the award. The seller should also take back the 15 machines.
2. The seller should compensate the buyer for the interest of US$156,820.59 on the total sum of the four L/Cs from 18 December 1988 to 30 June 1989.
3. The seller should refund the buyer the inspection fees of Rmb 15,928 paid by the buyer.
4. The seller should compensate the buyer for the legal costs of Rmb8,085.01.
 
The seller submitted the following defence:
 
1. The contract stipulated only that, `Output from the trial production should reach the contracted requirement that the quality of the output should meet international quality standards.' It was not stipulated that the imports should be advanced equipment and made to the standards of technology in the 1980s. Therefore,' year of production of the equipment imported was not important. The key point was whether the quantity and quality of production after installation of the equipment could reach the requirements for output stipulated under the contract. As the equipment delivered by the seller had not been installed and tested, it was still not known whether the quantity and quality of production using this equipment could satisfy the requirements of the contract. Further, from the buyer's letter dated 15 April 1989 to the China Commodities Inspection Company, it was clear that the claimant was well aware, before the import of the equipment and even at the time of signing the contract, that the equipment to be imported was secondhand and not brand new. The issue of a certificate of inspection by the CCIB did not comply with the agreement of the parties in the contract and contradicted the certificate of quality issued by the China Commodities Inspection Company. Therefore, the certificate of inspection should be null and void.
2. The L/Cs issued by the buyer contained many errors. The seller had no choice but to request that the buyer amend the L/Cs several times. The last amendment to the L/Cs was made on 19 April 1989. By this time, the delivery date stipulated in the contract had passed and the supplier had disposed of the equipment that the seller had ordered. It was after much effort that the seller was able to arrange for part of the equipment to be delivered to the destination. As the delay was caused by the buyer's errors in issuing the L/Cs, the buyer should bear the responsibility for the failure to import all of the equipment.
 
II. Views of the Arbitration Tribunal
 
After examining the application for arbitration, the defence and supplementary material submitted by the claimant and the respondent and listening to the representations and arguments of the parties at the hearing, the arbitration tribunal formed the following opinion:
1. After the claimant issued four L/Cs, the respondent had requested that the claimant amend certain wording and the content of the L/Cs. The claimant made the amendments accordingly. As time  was spent in the amending of the L/Cs, the parties agreed to extend the shipping period and the effective period of the L/Cs to 16 June 1989 and 7 July 1989. Thereafter, the respondent delivered part of the goods and used the four amended L/Cs to collect part of the cost of the goods as stipulated under the contract. As the respondent accepted the amended L/Cs, it had the obligation to supply the total quantity of goods within the extended shipping period. The respondent did not deliver to the claimant the total quantity of goods as stipulated in the contract and should be responsible for this. The respondent submitted that the failure to import all of the machines was due to the loss of the machines ordered by the respondent as a result of conflict between the respondent and its supplier which arose by reason of the time wasted in amending the L/Cs issued by the claimant. It believed, therefore, that the claimant should be responsible for the failure to deliver the total quantity of goods. The arbitration tribunal held that there were errors in the wording and content of the L/Cs issued by the claimant. The claimant amended these errors in accordance with the request of the respondent. Considering the time wasted in amending the L/Cs, the parties agreed to extend the shipping period and the effective period of the L/Cs. Under these circumstances, the rights of the respondent were not affected by the amendment of the L/Cs. The relationship between the respondent and its supplier had nothing to do with the claimant because the obligations of the claimant as the buyer under a normal trading contract should not be extended to include the seller's ability to obtain the goods. Therefore, the arbitration tribunal considered the above reasons submitted by the respondent could not be upheld.
2. The subject of dispute in the case was a production line comprising 69 machines. The respondent delivered only 15 of the machines to the claimant. As admitted by the respondent at the hearing, the 15 machines could not make up a complete production line. Because of this, the claimant did not enjoy the rights to which it was entitled under the contract. Therefore, the action of the respondent constituted a fundamental breach of the contract. As the 15 machines could not make the complete production line stipulated in the contract, it was no longer important whether the machines were brand new or secondhand.
3.  On the basis of items 1 and 2 above, the arbitration tribunal held that the request of the claimant for the goods to be returned was reasonable. The respondent should take back these machines, refund the payment made and pay the interest accrued to date on the payment after this payment was received by the respondent. The respondent received the large part of this payment before 7 July 1989. Only one payment of US$156,024.34 was received on 8 August 1989. Therefore, in calculating the interest payable by the respondent to the claimant, interest on the latter amount should be calculated from 9 August 1989. Interest on the other payments made by the claimant should be calculated from 7 July 1989, as requested by the claimant. The interest should cover the period ending 24 December 1990. The claimant requested a rate of one per cent per month, but the arbitration tribunal considered that this rate should be adjusted to be eight per cent per annum.
4.  In transactions involving L/Cs, banks usually ask the applicant to pay part or all of the amount under the L/C as a deposit. The deposit would normally be treated as a current deposit with an interest rate lower than that of other forms of deposit. Upon paying the deposit to the bank, the applicant bears the interest difference between a current deposit and other forms of deposits with respect to the deposit paid. Looking at the claimant's claim in its application for arbitration of the bank loan interest of US$156,482 on the total amount of US$2,550,000 under the four L/Cs for the period from 8 December 1988 to 30 June 1988, the representation and evidence submitted by the claimant showed that the claimant was a joint venture company. One of the shareholders of the claimant invested a loan granted by the bank into the joint venture company as equity capital. In the first place, therefore, the payment of interest on this loan by the shareholder to the bank was nothing to do with the claimant. In the second place, the loss of interest on the loan claimed by the claimant did not equal the difference between the interest on the deposit as a current deposit and its interest as another form of deposit that the bearer of the L/C could suffer after it had paid the deposit. Further, the claimant had not submitted evidence to prove that it had paid this deposit to the bank for the purpose of issuing the four L/Cs. In addition, it held that the respondent, as the seller under a normal trading contract, was not under obligation to consider whether the buyer had the ability to obtain a loan or how it would obtain the loan. The arbitration tribunal therefore considered that this claim by the claimant could not be upheld.
5. According to the relevant laws in China relating to commodities inspection at the time of importing the equipment, all commodities for import and export must undergo inspection. Therefore, the claimant must pay the inspection fees for the 15 machines. As the respondent was responsible for a fundamental breach of contract, the arbitration tribunal considered it reasonable for the respondent to compensate the claimant for the commodities inspection fees of Rmb15,928.
6.  The claimant also requested compensation from the respondent for legal costs of Rmb8,085.01. The arbitration tribunal considered that this cost was not directly or inevitably incurred by the breach of the respondent under normal circumstances. Therefore, this claim was not supported.
7.  The arbitration fees should be borne by the respondent.
 
III. The Decision
 
The arbitration tribunal decided as follows:
 
1.  The respondent shall refund to the claimant within 30 days of the date of this award the payment of US$1,145,425.60 made on the goods, plus interest of US$132,812.68, totalling US$1,278,238.28. Interest will be payable if the payment is not made on time.
2.  The respondent shall pay the claimant within 30 days of the date of this award the sum of Rmb15, 928. Interest will be payable if the payment is not made on time.
3.  The respondent shall within 60 days of the date of this award take back the 15 machines delivered to the claimant. The claimant has the obligation to assist the respondent in completion of the necessary formalities (including customs clearance) involved in the transfer of the goods. If the respondent does not take back the goods within the required time period, the claimant is entitled to dispose of the goods in accordance with the laws of China.
4.  The arbitration fees of US$15,863 and handling fees of US$1,476.83 shall be borne by the respondent. At the time of application for arbitration, the claimant had paid the arbitration fees and the handling fees. The respondent shall within 30 days of the date of this award pay the claimant US$17,339.83. Interest will be payable if the payment is not made on time.
 
DISCUSSION
 
According to the contract, the seller in this case should have delivered 69 machines that would form a production line. However, the seller delivered only 15 machines, which clearly constituted a fundamental breach of the contract. The arbitration tribunal decided, therefore, that the seller should refund the cost of the goods to the buyer, take back the goods and pay the commodities inspection fees. If the decision was enforced in full, the seller's loss would outweigh its gain. The buyer did not raise a claim for loss of profit. If it had done so, the arbitration tribunal would probably have awarded the claim and the seller would have lost even more.
At various times during the transaction, the seller had the chance to get out of the deal and claim compensation from the buyer. However, it let these opportunities slip in exchange for payment for the 15 machines.
According to the contract, the buyer was to issue L/Cs which fulfilled the requirements for the goods under the contract. This was a precondition for the seller's performance of its obligation to deliver the goods. It was agreed in the contract that the first batch of goods would be shipped on or before 10 March 1989. Assuming that the seller needed one month to book the shipping space and prepare for delivery, the buyer should have issued L/Cs pursuant to the contract before February 1989. The buyer issued the L/Cs on 18 December 1988, which was more than two months ahead of the shipping period agreed in the contract. The seller had enough time to book the shipping space and prepare for shipping. However, errors in the L/Cs issued by the buyer could be remedied by the buyer amending the L/Cs, but only provided that the seller had enough time to perform its delivery obligations after it received the amended L/Cs. The seller requested amendment of the L/Cs and the buyer complied.
If the amended L/Cs fulfilled the contract requirements, the seller would have had to perform its delivery obligations. Even amended, however, the L/Cs still did not fulfill the requirements under the contract. The seller again requested that the buyer amend the L/Cs. On 18 April 1989, after several amendments, the L/Cs finally satisfied the necessary requirements. By this time, however, the shipping period agreed to in the contract had already passed. The shipping period on the L/Cs were, therefore, also amended accordingly. During this period, from 9 February 1989 onwards, there were several times at which the seller could have terminated the contract on the grounds that the L/Cs issued by the buyer did not satisfy the contractual requirements, and claim compensation from the buyer. However, in an attempt to complete the transaction, the seller requested that the buyer repeatedly amend the L/Cs. By doing this, the seller not only lost the right to terminate the contract and claim damages from the buyer; it also had to perform its obligation to deliver all the goods under the contract. When the seller was finally unable to deliver the total transacted goods, it could not relieve itself of its responsibility to deliver the goods on the grounds of delayed shipping due to continued amendment of errors in the L/Cs by the buyer.
From the above description, it can be observed that disputes can be avoided if the buyer and the seller can perform their respective obligations during a transaction. If one of the parties does not fulfill its obligations in full, the other party should take reasonable action to remedy the situation. If one party chooses to request that the other continue to perform the contract and they do so within the grace period, the other party, while entitled to claim damages, must perform its own obligations under the contract. In the event that it does not, the first party can seek reasonable remedies to the breach. In this case, the seller could not put forward a defence on the grounds that the buyer had defaulted. Parties to a transaction must clearly pay a great deal of attention to defaults. If one party handles the default of the other party inappropriately, it will probably have to bear responsibility for default itself. This is the case with the seller here.
The dispute between the parties involved a matter of delivery of secondhand goods by the seller. This eventually became a non-issue because the arbitration tribunal concluded that the seller had only delivered part of the goods, which constituted a fundamental breach of the contract. If the seller had delivered the total goods required under the transaction, the issue of the equipment being secondhand would have been the focus of the dispute. The seller did not deny that the equipment was secondhand and produced evidence to prove that the buyer had known at the time of signing the contract that the equipment to be delivered by the seller would be secondhand. This evidence was highly favourable to the seller. Therefore, if the buyer had wanted brand new equipment, it should have included detailed provisions in the contract regarding the performance and year of production of the equipment.
It should also have included provisions in the contract and in the L/Cs that would have prevented the seller providing old equipment, making use of the terms of payment, which was to be made upon delivery of the documents. Though the adoption of preventive measures like these would involve extra cost, these costs would be negligible beside the possible losses. The important thing is that the buyer should not rest assured that everything will be exactly as it wants once the L/C has been issued. Sometimes the buyer who wants to buy an expensive machine receives a bundle of scrap iron after it has made payment to the seller. These cases are not uncommon, but losses of this kind are not unavoidable.
(Xu Sanqiao)
