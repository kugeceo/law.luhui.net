CASE 13

WHAT CONSTITUTES ACCEPTANCE IN DOCUMENT AGAINST ACCEPTANCE?
 Under terms of document against acceptance, does the promise to accept given by the payer designated in the contract constitute valid acceptance? If this is not valid acceptance, is the buyer responsible for payment for the goods? When the mediation decision of the court confirms the obligation of the payer designated under the contract to pay the balance for the goods owing, is the seller entitled to request payment of this balance from the buyer?
 
THE ARBITRATION DECISION
 
I. Facts of the Case
 
On 14 October 1987, the claimant and the respondent signed purchase contract No. (87) AlOID046, in the city of Shenzhen. It was agreed in the contract that the claimant would sell to the respondent 499,875 kg of glycerine at a unit price of US$0.90 per kg, C&F Shanghai. The total price of the goods amounted to US$449,887.50. Payment was to be made within 30 days of the date of acceptance of the draft. A city industrial and trading construction company (the industrial and Trading Company) was designated as the payer for the goods under the contract.
On 30 November 1987, the claimant shipped 499,875 kg of glycerine. In the inward collection notice dated 25 December 1987, issued by the Shenzhen Branch of the Bank of China, the Industrial and Trading Company promised to accept the draft to the amount of US$449,887.50 that was presented by the claimant in favour of the Industrial and Trading Company, and to pay this amount by 30 January 1988. On 17 June 1988, 12 October 1988 and 25 October 1988, the Industrial and Trading Company paid to the claimant a total of US$280,000. The balance of US$169,887.50 remained unpaid. In November 1988, the claimant sued the Industrial and Trading Company in an Intermediate People's Court. The Court took cognisance of the case and on 12 December 1988 made a mediation decision, Ref. No. (1988) XX Zhong Fa Jing Zi No. 302 (the Mediation Decision). As the Industrial and Trading Company did not act in accordance with the Mediation Decision, the claimant applied to the Court on 13 January 1989 and 26 January 1989 for enforcement. The Industrial and Trading Company paid a further US$30,000 to the claimant. The Industrial and Trading Company did not settle the balance of US$174,477.50 despite several further reminders from the claimant.
On 26 February 1990, the claimant submitted the case to the Shenzhen Branch of the Commission requesting that the respondent pay US$174,477.50 plus interest.
In its defence, dated 27 March 1990, the respondent requested that the arbitration clause in the contract be declared void and that the claim of the claimant be rejected. The respondent stated that the subject of the claimant's application for arbitration with the Commission was the dispute concerning the standing debt between the claimant and the Industrial and Trading Company. This dispute had been dealt with by the Court through the Mediation Decision, which confirmed that the Industrial and Trading Company was to pay the outstanding amount due to the claimant under the contract. It also claimed that it had applied to the Court for enforcement of the Mediation Decision. If the claimant had not yet received the total amount owing, the respondent claimed that the claimant should further urge the Court to enforce the Mediation Decision and should withdraw its application for arbitration, with the Commission.
The claimant submitted a counter-defence on 7 May 1990 and on 20 August 1990 made a further written representation through its agent. The claimant considered that the dispute between the claimant and the Industrial and Trading Company arose from problems with bills of exchange whereas the dispute between the claimant and the respondent arose from the sale and purchase contract. The two issues were legally separate, and the arbitration clause in the contract was valid.
 
II. Views of the Arbitration Tribunal
 
The arbitration tribunal held that:
 
1. The defence of the respondent explained only that there was a dispute between the claimant and the respondent over payment for the goods under the contract, but did not convince the arbitration tribunal that the arbitration clause in the contract was void. The arbitration tribunal stated that the arbitration clause in the contract was valid and that it had jurisdiction over disputes arising out of the contract.
2. In the contract, the claimant and the respondent agreed that the payment terms would be document against acceptance and designated the Industrial and Trading Company as the payer for the goods under the contract. When the Industrial and Trading Company accepted the draft, it only promised to the bank that it accepted the draft, which, as such, did not constitute the act of accepting the draft itself. There was no relationship arising from the bills of exchange between the claimant and the Industrial and Trading Company. Therefore, the obligation to pay that existed under the contract was not fulfilled. In light of this, the several , partial payments made under the contract by mail transfer and T/T by the Industrial and Trading Company, following its promise to accept the draft, could not be deemed either to be performance of its obligations under the draft, or an implication that it was the buyer under the contract. Rather, these acts could only be deemed to be performance of the obligations of the respondent, the buyer under the contract, to pay. As the Industrial and Trading Company had not fulfilled its obligation to pay on behalf of the respondent, the obligations of the respondent under the contract were not completely discharged; therefore, it still was responsible for fulfilling its obligation to pay the claimant for the goods under the contract.
3. However, when the Industrial and Trading. Company did not pay the full amount for the goods under the contract on behalf of the respondent, the claimant sued the Industrial and Trading Company. The Court took cognisance of the case, rendering a Mediation Decision, which had the effect of a judgment. The Mediation Decision confirmed that the Industrial and Trading Company was obliged to pay to the claimant the balance owing for the goods under the contract. As long as the Industrial and Trading Company was able to pay, the claimant's right to claim the balance due under the contract from the Industrial and Trading Company was protected. Although the respondent had not been discharged of its obligation under the contract to pay, the claimant should not be paid twice for the same amount, as this would amount to the claimant earning profits to which it was not entitled under the law.
4. Therefore, although the respondent was still bound under the contract to pay the outstanding balance for the goods due to the claimant, the claimant could obtain this sum from the Industrial and Trading Company pursuant to the Mediation Decision. The arbitration tribunal was of the opinion that it could not make an award that demanded that the respondent pay the balance to the claimant again.
5. As the arbitration proceedings were initiated by the claimant, the arbitration fees for the case should be borne by the claimant.
 
III. The Decision
 
The arbitration tribunal decided that:
 
1. The claimant's request that the respondent pay US$174,477.50, plus interest shall be rejected.
2. The arbitration and handling fees for the case shall be borne by the claimant. These fees had been offset by the amount paid by the claimant to the Shenzhen Branch of the Commission upon application for arbitration.
 
This decision shall be final.
 
DISCUSSION
 
In their contract, the parties designated the Industrial and Trading Company as the payer for the goods and agreed payment terms of settlement within 30 days of the date of acceptance of the draft. The respondent signed an import agency contract with the Industrial and Trading Company for the provision of glycerine importing services at a commission of 1.5 per cent of the total price of the goods imported, and the amount due under the purchase contract was to be paid by the Industrial and Trading Company.
The mechanism for the collection of payment was based on credit. There are two types of collection of payment, namely, documents against payment and documents against acceptance. The latter method was adopted in this case. Documents against acceptance entails the collecting bank's release of documents to the buyer only upon acceptance of these documents by the buyer. An acceptance is a written undertaking made by the buyer to honour a draft for a future date agreed to by the buyer and the seller. Following acceptance by the buyer, the bank will give the buyer the documents as proof of its title to the goods. If the buyer goes bankrupt or becomes insolvent before payment for the goods has been settled, the seller will suffer losses. Therefore, a seller seldom accepts the terms of document against acceptance unless the buyer's creditworthiness is unquestionable or there has been a proven long-term relationship between the buyer and the seller.
Following delivery of the goods on 30 November 1987, the claimant issued a draft for US$449,887.50 for the account of the Industrial and Trading Company specifying the Hong Kong Branch of the National Commercial Bank Ltd as the receiving bank. On 25 December 1987, the Shenzhen Branch of the Bank of China, as the collecting bank, sent the following inward collection notice to the Industrial and Trading Company:
Dear Sirs, We have received from the Domestic and Overseas (Hong Kong and Macau) Bank the collection documents under your contract. We now enclose an invoice for your reference. The reference number of the receiving bank is National Commercial Bank is J090E-32495 and the reference number of the collecting bank is IC450870850. The invoiced amount totals US$449,887.50. Please check and accept or reject these documents as soon as possible. Others: D/A document against acceptance within 30 days. Collection fees to be borne by your company.
The Industrial and Trading Company marked at the foot of the notice, ‘Agree to accept: Payment to be made between 1 January 1988 and 30 January 1988.’ The financial stamp of the Industrial and Trading Company was affixed upon the notice. Upon producing this notice, the Industrial and Trading Company collected the documents and took delivery of the goods. However, it did not make full payment for the goods under the contract when payment was due and only paid a total of US$280,000 in installments on 17 June 1988,12 October 1988 and 25 October 1988.
It is important to clarify whether the wording, ‘Agree to accept. Payment to be made’, marked by the Industrial and Trading Company on the inward collection notice constituted an act of acceptance. The claimant consistently held the view that its dispute with the Industrial and Trading Company was not about payment of the contract price for the goods under a purchase contract but about the acceptance of bills of exchange. It considered that the dispute arose because the Industrial and Trading Company did not observe the laws regarding bills of exchange. At the hearing, the respondent also agreed that the claimant and the Industrial and Trading Company were legally related by certain bills of exchange but claimed that this relationship could not be considered to be independent of the sales and purchase contract.
Careful examination of the case shows that the parties misinterpreted this issue. Normally, it is not required by law that the holder of the bill of exchange show the bill to the payer to request acceptance. However, if the bill is to be paid within a fixed period after sight, acceptance is necessary to enable confirmation of the date of payment. The holder is then required to show the bill to the payer for acceptance. Acceptance usually involves signature on the draft by the payer, the writing of the word ‘accept’ on the front of the draft and the writing of the date of acceptance. Most important is the signature of the payer. The draft cannot be deemed to have been accepted if it is not signed by the buyer.
 International requirements regarding the writing of the word `accept' and the date of acceptance may differ. Under the laws of some countries, which include Britain and the United States, acceptance is confirmed simply by the signature of the acceptor. Marking the word `accept' on the bill is not required. Most countries do not require a date of acceptance as a prerequisite for the acceptance to be effective. The Geneva Convention on Uniform Laws, however, provides that acceptance of bills must be evidenced by the word `accept' and that certain bills require a date of acceptance. According to the principles of law on bills of exchange, a person will be liable under a bill of exchange only after he has signed (accepted) the bill of exchange and has undertaken the obligation to pay. In this case, the Industrial and Trading Company only marked the words; ‘Agree to accept’, on the inward collection notice issued by the collecting bank and did not sign the draft drawn by the claimant. Legally speaking, therefore, this did not constitute an act of acceptance under the law on bills of exchange. As the arbitration tribunal explained: ‘When the Industrial and Trading Company accepted the draft, it only promised to the bank to accept the draft. However, the promise to accept the draft did not constitute the act of accepting the draft itself. There was no relationship arising from the bills of exchange between the claimant and the Industrial and Trading Company.’ The arbitration tribunal deemed the action of the Industrial and Trading Company to be a ‘promise’. A promise to accept is not actual acceptance.
The parties confused these two concepts and therefore mistakenly concluded that the claimant and the Industrial and Trading Company were legally related by the bills of exchange.
It is, however, surprising that a promise to accept was even given. This could have involved a mistake or ignorance on the part of the collecting bank or on the part of the Industrial and Trading Company.
Once the relationship between the claimant and the Industrial and Trading Company is clarified, how should the respondent's responsibility to pay as the buyer in this transaction be determined? As no legal relationship based on bills of exchange existed between the claimant and the Industrial and Trading Company, the payment of US$280,000 in three installments to the claimant by the Industrial and Trading Company could not be deemed fulfillment of its obligation to pay under the bills of exchange, but only as performance of the obligation of the respondent, as buyer under the contract on behalf of the respondent, to pay. As the buyer in the contract, the respondent was obliged to accept and pay for the goods under the contract. When the payer specified in the contract did not fulfilll its obligation to pay, the responsibility to pay fell back on the buyer.
When the claimant did not receive the entire amount that was due to it under the contract, it could have protected its interests by proposing to the buyer that the case be submitted to arbitration pursuant to the arbitration clause of the contract and requesting that the buyer pay the outstanding balance of US$169,887.50. Instead; it sued the Industrial and Trading Company in court for its delay in honoring the draft. The Court took cognisance of the case and called the parties for mediation. The parties reached a mediation agreement voluntarily and the Court made the Mediation Decision ruling on 12 December 1988. The major terms of the settlement were that the Industrial and Trading Company was to pay to the claimant the outstanding amount of US$169,887.50 together with interest of US$34,000 accruing from 30 January 1988 to the date of payment; the Industrial and Trading Company would pay the amount in three installments after the Mediation Decision took effect; and the first installment of US$30,000, the second installment of US$40,000 and the third installment of the outstanding balance were to be settled within three, 45 and 90 days, respectively, from the date of the Mediation Decision.
According to the Provisional Civil Procedure Law of the People's Republic of China (the Provisional Civil Procedural Law) then in force, a mediation decision is effective from the moment it is served and has the legal effect of a judgment. That is to say, the parties involved in a mediation decision must act according to the mediation decision. When one party refuses to comply with the decision, the other party can apply to the People's Court to have the decision enforced. In this case, after the Mediation Decision became effective, the Industrial and Trading Company did not comply with the rulings of the decision. The claimant applied to the People's Court on 13 January 1989 and 26 January 1989 for the decision to be enforced, after which, on 10 March 1989 the Industrial and Trading Company paid only the first installment of US$30,000. The second and third installments remained unpaid. By February 1990, the claimant had still not received the outstanding installments. It was at this time that the claimant submitted the case to arbitration and claimed the overdue balance for the goods from the buyer.
Another issue arises here. The Mediation Decision confirmed that the Industrial and Trading Company had the obligation to pay the amount due, so was the seller also entitled to request that this buyer pay this same amount? As noted above, once a mediation decision is effective, an application can be made to the courts for enforcement of the decision when one party does not comply with the decision. The outstanding debt for payment of the goods owed by the Industrial and Trading Company to the claimant was confirmed by the Mediation Decision, and the claimant eventually applied to the court for the decision to be enforced. According to the Provisional Civil Procedure Law, the presiding officer has to investigate a case within ten days of receipt of an application for enforcement of a decision and direct the party subject to the enforcement to act in accordance with this decision within a specified period of time. Non-performance within this period will lead to measures being taken for enforcement of the decision. These measures may include detention, withdrawal of the savings or income of the defendant, or the sealing, detention, freezing or realization of the assets of the party subject to the enforcement. The savings of companies and organizations subject to enforcement will be transferred or transmitted by banks or credit co-operatives pursuant to the notice of assistance in enforcement issued by the People's Court.
The rights of a claimant as a creditor are therefore protected by Chinese law under these provisions. When the Industrial and Trading Company did not perform its obligations in accordance with the Mediation Decision, the Court was entitled to take measures to enforce the decision to enable the claimant to retrieve the outstanding sum that was owed to it. Was the respondent then relieved of its obligation as the buyer under the contract to pay? The answer is no. As the payment due was only made in part, if the Industrial and Trading Company became insolvent or went. bankrupt, the claimant could still request that the respondent pay the outstanding amount even though a mediation decision between the Industrial and Trading Company and the claimant existed. The respondent could therefore only be discharged of its obligation to pay once the Industrial and Trading Company had settled the entire amount due. In its defence, the respondent claimed that its responsibility as an import agent had been erased because the import agency contract between the claimant and the respondent, the repayment agreement between the claimant and the Industrial and
Trading Company and the civil Mediation Decision of the Court clearly confirmed the Industrial and Trading Company's obligation to pay. The respondent's reasoning was incorrect because as long as part of the outstanding payment remained unpaid by the Industrial and Trading Company, the respondent was liable should a claim be made.
The claimant applied to the Court for enforcement of the part of the Mediation Decision that remained unperformed. The Court had not enforced this decision at the time the claimant submitted the case for arbitration. As the claimant could not produce evidence that the failure to enforce was due to the insolvency or bankruptcy of the Industrial and Trading Company, the arbitration tribunal could only hold that the rights of the claimant as a creditor were protected and, as such, it could still obtain the outstanding balance from the Industrial and Trading Company pursuant to the
Mediation Decision. The arbitration tribunal could not award payment of the outstanding balance by the respondent to the claimant because, as the arbitration tribunal ruled, the claimant ‘should not be paid twice for the same amount, otherwise it would be gaining extra profits which should not be protected by law.’

The arbitration tribunal was not, therefore, in a position to accept the request of the claimant and could only dismiss it. The only remedy available to the claimant was to enforce the Mediation Decision against the Industrial and Trading Company through the Court according to legal procedure. If the Industrial and Trading Company failed to pay the outstanding amount, even after it entered liquidation proceedings or was declared bankrupt, the claimant could then reapply for arbitration and claim damages from the respondent in accordance with the contract.
